Assigned Reading Notes

Reading scopeThese notes map the Module 6 learning outcomes to the assigned McCance readings and the urinary incontinence quality-of-life paper.

McCance 9th ed. pp. 843-848, 1232-1241, 1281-1282: Obstruction, BPH, Prostatitis, Pediatric Incontinence

  • Urinary tract obstruction blocks urine flow anywhere from renal tubules to urethra; location determines whether damage is unilateral/bilateral and upper/lower tract.
  • Upper tract obstruction can cause hydroureter, hydronephrosis, increased tubular pressure, reduced glomerular filtration, ischemia, infection risk, and kidney damage.
  • Lower tract obstruction can produce bladder distention, urinary retention, detrusor hypertrophy, overflow incontinence, recurrent UTI, reflux, hydroureter, and hydronephrosis.
  • BPH narrows the prostatic urethra and increases bladder outlet resistance, producing hesitancy, weak stream, intermittency, straining, incomplete emptying, frequency, nocturia, urgency, retention, and overflow.
  • Prostatitis may cause pelvic/perineal pain, dysuria, frequency, fever or systemic symptoms in acute infection, and chronic pain or recurrent urinary symptoms in chronic forms.
  • Urinary incontinence affects quality of life through embarrassment, activity restriction, sleep disruption, skin irritation, falls risk, caregiver burden, and social isolation.

McCance 9th ed. pp. 1242-1245, 1280-1281: UTI Types and Pediatric UTI

  • Cystitis is lower urinary tract infection involving the bladder; pyelonephritis is upper tract infection involving renal pelvis and kidney parenchyma.
  • Most uncomplicated UTIs are ascending infections from periurethral flora; Escherichia coli is the most common causative organism.
  • Other organisms can include Klebsiella, Proteus, Enterococcus, Staphylococcus saprophyticus, and health-care associated gram-negative organisms.
  • Risk factors include female anatomy, sexual activity, pregnancy, urinary stasis, obstruction, catheterization, diabetes, older age, vesicoureteral reflux, and immunosuppression.
  • Cystitis commonly causes dysuria, urgency, frequency, suprapubic pain, cloudy or malodorous urine, and sometimes hematuria.
  • Pyelonephritis adds fever, chills, flank pain, costovertebral angle tenderness, nausea/vomiting, malaise, and sepsis risk.

McCance 9th ed. pp. 1245-1251, 1275-1278: Glomerulonephritis and Syndromes

  • Glomerulonephritis is immune-mediated glomerular inflammation that injures capillary walls, mesangium, podocytes, or basement membrane.
  • Acute risk contexts include postinfectious immune response, IgA nephropathy, lupus nephritis, vasculitis, anti-GBM disease, and infection-associated immune complex disease.
  • Chronic glomerulonephritis can follow persistent or repeated glomerular injury and progresses through sclerosis, nephron loss, and chronic kidney disease.
  • Nephritic syndrome reflects inflammatory glomerular injury: hematuria, red blood cell casts, reduced GFR, oliguria, azotemia, hypertension, and mild to moderate proteinuria.
  • Nephrotic syndrome reflects podocyte/filtration barrier injury: heavy proteinuria, hypoalbuminemia, edema, hyperlipidemia, lipiduria, and thromboembolism/infection risk.

McCance 9th ed. pp. 1252-1260: AKI, CKD, and ESRD

  • AKI is abrupt decline in kidney function with rising creatinine, reduced urine output, impaired water/electrolyte/acid-base regulation, and toxin retention.
  • Prerenal AKI is decreased renal perfusion; intrinsic AKI is kidney tissue injury; postrenal AKI is obstruction to urine outflow.
  • CKD is persistent kidney damage or reduced GFR over time, commonly caused by diabetes, hypertension, chronic glomerular disease, polycystic kidney disease, recurrent pyelonephritis, obstruction, or nephrotoxins.
  • CKD progression involves nephron loss, compensatory hyperfiltration in remaining nephrons, glomerular hypertension, sclerosis, fibrosis, and declining renal reserve.
  • ESRD is kidney failure severe enough to require kidney replacement therapy or transplantation for survival.
  • Major manifestations include fluid overload, hypertension, electrolyte abnormalities, metabolic acidosis, uremia, anemia, mineral-bone disorder, pruritus, fatigue, nausea, and neurologic changes.

Lecture and Clinical Presentation Notes

Nephron and Filtration Basics

  • Filtration occurs as materials in blood pass through glomerular capillaries into Bowman's capsule.
  • Glomerular capillaries are fenestrated and work with podocytes to regulate filtration.
  • The endothelial cells and basement membrane are negatively charged, helping repel negatively charged plasma proteins.
  • Tubular cells can also secrete materials into filtrate for elimination.
  • Filtrate moves through the collecting duct to renal papillae, then drains into minor calyces, major calyces, renal pelvis, ureter, and bladder.

Renal Function Testing

  • Urinalysis evaluates colour, turbidity, protein, pH, specific gravity, sediment, and supernatant.
  • Normal urine is clear and straw-coloured, with slight odour, some crystals, a small number of lining cells, and transparent hyaline casts.
  • Normal urine should not contain sugars, yeast cells, protein, ketones, bacteria, or parasitic organisms.
  • Normal urine pH range highlighted in the lecture: 4.5 to 8.0.
  • Specific gravity reflects solute concentration; lecture range highlighted: 1.0005 to 1.035.

Microscopic Hematuria

  • Microscopic hematuria is defined as three or more red blood cells per high-power field on microscopic urinalysis.
  • Dipstick blood is a screening result only; confirmation requires microscopic urinalysis.
  • Hematuria is a sign, not a disease. Causes can include malignancy, renal disease, infection, stones, trauma, instrumentation, exercise, menstruation, medications, or systemic disease.
  • Glomerular hematuria is suggested by dysmorphic RBCs, proteinuria, red blood cell casts, elevated creatinine, or other renal disease signs.
  • Non-glomerular hematuria usually has normally shaped RBCs without proteinuria or cellular casts and can arise anywhere along the urinary tract.
  • High-yield risk categories from the consult: obstruction, infection, genetic renal disease, hematologic disease, metabolic disease, malignancy, trauma, and benign/transient causes.
  • Potential glomerular injury outcomes include hematuria plus proteinuria, edema, hypertension, CKD, and end-stage renal failure.

Diabetes, SGLT2, and Kidney Protection

  • CKD requires structural or functional kidney abnormality lasting more than three months.
  • Diabetic kidney disease combines hemodynamic injury and metabolic injury: hyperfiltration, mesangial matrix expansion, podocyte damage/loss, glomerulosclerosis, and tubulointerstitial fibrosis.
  • SGLT2 transporters in the proximal convoluted tubule reabsorb about 90% of filtered glucose with sodium.
  • In diabetes, increased proximal sodium/glucose reabsorption reduces sodium delivery to the macula densa, disrupting tubuloglomerular feedback and promoting hyperfiltration.
  • The macula densa reads low NaCl as a false low-filtration signal, reducing adenosine, dilating the afferent arteriole, increasing angiotensin signaling, and constricting the efferent arteriole.
  • SGLT2 inhibitors block proximal glucose/sodium reabsorption, increase glucosuria and NaCl delivery to the macula densa, restore tubuloglomerular feedback, and reduce glomerular capillary pressure.
  • Metabolic injury pathways highlighted in the consult include advanced glycation end products, toxic non-glycolytic pathways, mitochondrial burnout, altered gene expression, oxidative stress, inflammatory cytokines, and renal fibrosis.
  • Patient teaching: expect glucosuria, genital/urinary symptoms, mild diuretic effect; teach hydration, hygiene, and sick-day holding during dehydration/GI illness risk.

Clinical Workup Pearls

  • If dipstick shows blood, confirm with microscopic urinalysis; do not diagnose microscopic hematuria from dipstick alone.
  • First rule out transient causes such as vigorous exercise, menstruation, or infection, then repeat microscopy after the trigger resolves.
  • Initial primary care assessment should include history, physical exam, blood pressure, creatinine, BUN, and calculated GFR.
  • Urology referral was emphasized for symptomatic hematuria, age 35 or older, or younger patients with malignancy risks such as smoking, pelvic radiation, occupational hazard exposure, or carcinogenic alkylating-agent exposure.
  • Urological evaluation may include cystoscopy, upper urinary tract imaging, and urinary cytology when indicated.
  • Nephrology referral is needed when urine suggests glomerular disease: dysmorphic RBCs, proteinuria, cellular casts, or renal impairment.
  • After negative specialty work-up, primary care monitoring includes symptom surveillance and repeat microscopic urinalysis over time.

Exam-Style Distinctions

  • UACR is useful as a baseline and monitoring tool in patients at risk for kidney disease.
  • uACR of 30 mg/g or higher is a CKD marker; GFR below 60 mL/min/1.73 m2 for more than three months also meets CKD criteria.
  • Uremic syndrome from toxin accumulation usually needs nephrology consultation.
  • Alkaline urine above pH 7.0 increases calcium phosphate and struvite stone risk; acidic urine below pH 5.0 increases uric acid stone risk.
  • Low-calcium diets can increase calcium oxalate stone risk because less calcium binds oxalate in the gut.
  • Older adults with UTI may present with confusion or vague abdominal discomfort instead of classic urinary symptoms.
  • Flank or groin pain helps distinguish pyelonephritis from lower UTI.

Core Lecture Deck Add-Ins

  • Bladder basics: urine storage occurs in the bladder; detrusor muscle contraction causes micturition; internal and external sphincters regulate voiding.
  • Urine sediment: red cell casts suggest bleeding in tubules; white cell casts suggest inflammation; epithelial cell casts suggest degradation or necrosis.
  • Aging kidney: kidney size, renal blood flow, GFR, nephron number, tubular transport, and reabsorption decline with age.
  • Aging bladder/renal changes can contribute to urgency, frequency, and nocturia.
  • Obstruction severity depends on location, one-sided versus bilateral involvement, completeness, duration, and cause.

Obstruction and Failure Deck Cues

  • Urinary obstruction increases pressure in Bowman's capsule, decreases renal filtration, promotes urinary stasis, and increases infection risk.
  • Nephrolithiasis formation requires salt supersaturation, precipitation, and growth by crystallization or aggregation; formation is pH- and temperature-dependent.
  • Kidney stone manifestations: renal colic, nausea, vomiting, hematuria, urgency, and frequent voiding.
  • Renal insufficiency means renal function has declined to about 25% of normal; end-stage renal failure means less than 10% remains.
  • CKD clinical manifestations often do not appear until renal function declines to less than 25% of normal.

Nephron Must-Knows

  • Filtrate flow: glomerulus/Bowman's capsule -> PCT -> loop of Henle -> DCT -> collecting duct -> renal pelvis -> ureter.
  • Four urine-formation processes: filtration, reabsorption, secretion, and excretion.
  • Most nutrient and solute reabsorption occurs in the proximal convoluted tubule.
  • PCT reabsorbs most sodium and water plus glucose, amino acids, bicarbonate, phosphate, and other filtered nutrients.
  • PCT also secretes hydrogen ions, organic anions/cations, drugs, and toxins.

Filtration Barrier

  • Filtered: water, electrolytes, glucose, urea, and creatinine.
  • Not normally filtered: red blood cells, platelets, and large proteins.
  • Barrier protection depends on size selectivity and negative charge selectivity.
  • Podocytes maintain filtration slit diaphragms, prevent protein loss, and preserve selective permeability.
  • Podocyte injury causes proteinuria and is central to nephrotic syndrome.

Obstruction Practice Case

  • Case pattern: 42-year-old male with intermittent lower back pain, difficulty urinating, nausea, and gross hematuria.
  • Likely diagnosis from the photo notes: renal calculi causing urinary obstruction.
  • Differentials to keep in mind: UTI, pyelonephritis, BPH, renal malignancy, and broader urinary obstruction.
  • Age-pattern cues: children often congenital abnormalities; young adults often renal calculi; older adults stones, tumors, strictures, or BPH.
  • Complete obstruction can damage renal tubules within hours; permanent damage may occur within 3 to 4 weeks; recovery after relief may take up to 3 months.

CKD and Uremic Syndrome

  • CKD is progressive, irreversible loss of kidney function.
  • Uremic syndrome is systemic toxicity from severe kidney failure.
  • Neurologic: lethargy, seizures, coma.
  • GI: nausea, vomiting, anorexia.
  • Cardiovascular/respiratory: hypertension, pericarditis, heart failure, dyspnea, pleurisy.
  • Other: anemia, bruising, epistaxis, pruritus, sallow skin, uremic frost, neuropathy, reproductive dysfunction, edema, bone pain, nail changes.

2. Urinary Tract Infection

Cystitis

  • Lower UTI of the bladder, usually caused by ascending periurethral organisms.
  • Escherichia coli is most common; Staphylococcus saprophyticus is a classic uncomplicated cystitis cause in young sexually active patients.
  • Symptoms: dysuria, frequency, urgency, suprapubic discomfort, cloudy/malodorous urine, hematuria.
  • Fever, flank pain, systemic illness, or vomiting suggests upper tract disease or complicated infection.

Pyelonephritis

  • Upper UTI involving renal pelvis and parenchyma.
  • Often ascends from bladder infection; obstruction, reflux, pregnancy, diabetes, and catheters increase risk.
  • Symptoms: fever, chills, flank pain, CVA tenderness, nausea/vomiting, malaise, urinary symptoms.
  • Complications: bacteremia, sepsis, renal scarring, abscess, AKI, preterm complications in pregnancy.

3. Glomerular Disorders

Acute and Chronic Glomerulonephritis

  • Immune complexes, antibodies, complement activation, or inflammatory cells injure glomeruli.
  • Acute forms often follow infection or systemic immune disease and can produce abrupt hematuria, oliguria, edema, and hypertension.
  • Chronic forms involve progressive scarring, nephron loss, proteinuria/hematuria, hypertension, and CKD progression.
  • Risk contexts: streptococcal infection, viral infection, lupus, vasculitis, anti-GBM disease, IgA nephropathy, diabetes, hypertension, and family history.

AKI Manifestations

  • Oliguria or nonoliguric reduced clearance, rising creatinine/BUN, fluid overload, edema, hypertension.
  • Hyperkalemia, metabolic acidosis, uremic symptoms, nausea, fatigue, confusion, pericarditis in severe uremia.
  • Assessment cue: separate perfusion problem, intrinsic renal injury, and obstruction quickly because management differs.

CKD and ESRD Manifestations

  • Long-term nephron loss causes reduced renal reserve, sodium/water retention, hypertension, and progressive toxin retention.
  • Common causes: diabetes, hypertension, chronic glomerular disease, polycystic kidney disease, recurrent pyelonephritis, obstruction, nephrotoxins.
  • Complications: anemia from low erythropoietin, mineral-bone disorder, metabolic acidosis, hyperkalemia, pruritus, malnutrition, uremia, cardiovascular disease.
  • ESRD requires dialysis or transplant when kidney function cannot maintain fluid, electrolyte, acid-base, and toxin balance.

Clinical Practice Quick Cards

Urinary History

  • Dysuria, urgency, frequency, nocturia, hesitancy, weak stream, incomplete emptying, retention.
  • Hematuria, flank pain, suprapubic pain, fever/chills, nausea/vomiting, stone history.
  • Leak timing: urge, cough/sneeze/exertion, dribbling, mobility/cognition barriers.
  • Meds: NSAIDs, ACEi/ARB, diuretics, antibiotics, anticholinergics, opioids, contrast exposure.

Renal Red Flags

  • Fever plus flank pain, sepsis signs, pregnancy with UTI symptoms, immunocompromised state.
  • Anuria, acute retention, severe colic, solitary kidney, obstruction with infection.
  • Gross hematuria, new edema/hypertension with cola-colored urine, rapidly rising creatinine.
  • Hyperkalemia symptoms, pulmonary edema, uremic confusion, pericarditis, severe acidosis.

Focused Assessment

  • Vitals, volume status, weight change, edema, lung crackles, orthostatic symptoms.
  • Abdominal/suprapubic distention, CVA tenderness, bladder scan/post-void residual when indicated.
  • Urinalysis pattern: leukocytes/nitrites, blood, protein, casts, specific gravity.
  • Labs and imaging based on pattern: creatinine/eGFR, electrolytes, CBC, culture, ultrasound/CT, protein quantification.

Practice and Active Recall

1. What is the fastest distinction between upper and lower urinary tract obstruction?
Upper obstruction involves the renal pelvis or ureter and often causes flank pain/hydronephrosis; lower obstruction involves bladder outlet or urethra and causes voiding symptoms, retention, overflow, and possible back pressure.
2. Why can BPH lead to hydronephrosis?
BPH increases bladder outlet resistance, causing retention and high bladder pressure that can reflux/back up into ureters and kidneys.
3. How do urge and stress incontinence differ?
Urge incontinence is sudden urge with detrusor overactivity; stress incontinence is leakage during increased intra-abdominal pressure from weak sphincter or pelvic support.
4. What pattern suggests overflow incontinence?
Dribbling, weak stream, hesitancy, incomplete emptying, and high post-void residual suggest retention with overflow.
5. What organism most commonly causes uncomplicated UTI?
Escherichia coli is the most common causative organism.
6. What findings push cystitis toward pyelonephritis?
Fever, chills, flank pain, CVA tenderness, nausea/vomiting, malaise, or sepsis signs suggest upper tract infection.
7. Nephritic vs nephrotic: what is the quickest comparison?
Nephritic is inflammatory with hematuria, RBC casts, reduced GFR, oliguria, and hypertension; nephrotic is heavy protein loss with hypoalbuminemia, edema, hyperlipidemia, and lipiduria.
8. What are the three categories of AKI?
Prerenal from low perfusion, intrinsic from kidney tissue injury, and postrenal from urine outflow obstruction.
9. How does CKD progress after nephron loss?
Remaining nephrons hyperfilter, increasing glomerular pressure and sclerosis, which causes more nephron loss, fibrosis, and declining renal reserve.
10. Why does CKD cause anemia and bone-mineral problems?
Reduced erythropoietin lowers red blood cell production; impaired phosphate excretion and vitamin D activation disrupt calcium/phosphate balance and bone metabolism.
11. What confirms microscopic hematuria?
Microscopic urinalysis showing three or more red blood cells per high-power field. Dipstick blood is only a screen.
12. Which urine findings suggest glomerular hematuria?
Dysmorphic RBCs, red blood cell casts, proteinuria, elevated creatinine, or other renal disease signs.
13. How do SGLT2 inhibitors reduce diabetic hyperfiltration?
They block proximal glucose/sodium reabsorption, increase sodium delivery to the macula densa, restore tubuloglomerular feedback, and reduce glomerular capillary pressure.
14. Why can NSAIDs worsen kidney perfusion?
They reduce prostaglandin production; prostaglandins help dilate afferent arterioles and maintain renal blood flow when perfusion is stressed.
15. Why is vesicoureteral reflux important in children?
Reflux plus lower UTI can carry bacteria upward, causing pyelonephritis and increasing risk for renal scarring.
16. When should hematuria prompt nephrology referral?
When there are signs of glomerular disease such as dysmorphic RBCs, proteinuria, cellular casts, or renal impairment.
17. What urine pH patterns matter for stone risk?
Alkaline urine above pH 7.0 increases calcium phosphate and struvite stone risk; acidic urine below pH 5.0 increases uric acid stone risk.
18. Why can low-calcium diets worsen calcium oxalate stone risk?
Less dietary calcium binds oxalate in the gut, so more oxalate can be absorbed and excreted in urine.
19. How might UTI present differently in older adults?
Older adults may present with confusion or vague abdominal discomfort rather than classic dysuria, urgency, or frequency.
20. What should patients on SGLT2 inhibitors know about sick days?
They may need to temporarily hold the medication during acute GI illness, dehydration risk, or poor intake to reduce AKI and rare DKA risk, following clinician guidance.
21. What is the correct order of major nephron structures for filtrate flow?
Glomerulus/Bowman's capsule -> proximal convoluted tubule -> loop of Henle -> distal convoluted tubule -> collecting duct.
22. What is the high-yield AKI recognition triad from the photo notes?
Increased serum creatinine, decreased GFR, and decreased urine output.
23. What is the difference between azotemia and uremia?
Azotemia is elevated nitrogenous wastes such as BUN/creatinine as a lab abnormality; uremia is symptomatic toxicity from waste accumulation.
24. In the 42-year-old male case with back pain, urinary difficulty, nausea, and gross hematuria, what diagnosis fits best?
Renal calculi causing urinary obstruction.
25. What is the most common cause of CKD in the photo notes?
Diabetes mellitus, through diabetic glomerulopathy.
26. What are mesangial cells responsible for?
They phagocytose debris, support podocytes, regulate GFR through contraction, produce cytokines/growth factors, and participate in immune responses.
27. What is the diabetic kidney disease hemodynamic sequence involving SGLT2?
Chronic hyperglycemia increases filtered glucose and proximal SGLT2 sodium/glucose reabsorption, lowering NaCl delivery to the macula densa. The kidney interprets this as low filtration, causing afferent dilation and efferent constriction, which raises glomerular pressure and drives hyperfiltration injury.
28. Why are SGLT2 inhibitors kidney-protective beyond glucose lowering?
They increase NaCl delivery to the macula densa, restore tubuloglomerular feedback, reduce afferent dilation/efferent constriction signaling, lower glomerular capillary pressure, and reduce hyperfiltration stress.
29. Which metabolic pathways contribute to diabetic kidney damage?
Advanced glycation end products, toxic non-glycolytic pathways such as aldose reductase and hexosamine pathways, mitochondrial burnout, altered gene expression, oxidative stress, inflammatory cytokines, and renal fibrosis all contribute to podocyte injury, mesangial expansion, glomerulosclerosis, and tubulointerstitial fibrosis.
30. What separates glomerular from non-glomerular microscopic hematuria?
Glomerular hematuria shows evidence of filtration-barrier injury such as dysmorphic RBCs, red cell casts, proteinuria, elevated creatinine, or renal disease signs. Non-glomerular hematuria usually has normal RBCs without proteinuria or casts and arises from the urinary tract outside the glomeruli.
31. What should happen after a dipstick shows blood?
Confirm with microscopic urinalysis. Microscopic hematuria is defined as three or more RBCs per high-power field, so dipstick alone is not diagnostic.
32. What findings prompt urology versus nephrology referral in microscopic hematuria?
Urology is emphasized for symptomatic hematuria, age 35 or older, or younger patients with malignancy risks such as smoking, pelvic radiation, occupational hazards, or carcinogenic alkylating-agent exposure. Nephrology is prompted by dysmorphic RBCs, proteinuria, cellular casts, or renal impairment.
