Hematology Foundations

Red Cells

Erythropoietin stimulates marrow erythropoiesis. Hemoglobin requires iron-containing heme plus globin chains; mature RBCs circulate about 120 days.

Primary Hemostasis

Platelet adhesion, activation, secretion, and aggregation create the initial platelet plug.

Secondary Hemostasis

Coagulation generates thrombin, which converts fibrinogen to fibrin; factor XIII cross-links fibrin to stabilize the plug.

Fast CBC approachConfirm anemia, classify by MCV, review reticulocyte response, smear, RDW, iron/B12/folate studies, hemolysis markers, renal/inflammatory context, and evidence of bleeding or marrow failure.

Essential Thrombocythemia

A clonal myeloproliferative neoplasm, commonly driven by JAK2, CALR, or MPL signalling, causes sustained megakaryocyte proliferation and thrombocytosis. Patients can develop arterial/venous thrombosis, microvascular symptoms, or paradoxical bleeding from acquired von Willebrand dysfunction at extreme platelet counts.

Adult vs Pediatric NHL

Adults have a broad range including indolent follicular and aggressive diffuse large B-cell lymphoma. Pediatric NHL is more often high grade, rapidly proliferative, and extranodal, with Burkitt, lymphoblastic, and anaplastic large-cell patterns prominent. Rapid growth can create tumour-lysis risk, but many pediatric subtypes are highly treatment-responsive.

Clinical Consult Add-Ins

Microcytic Hypochromic Anemia

  • Microcytosis reflects impaired hemoglobin synthesis; MCV is usually below 80 fL and cells appear pale from low hemoglobin content.
  • Iron deficiency is not a final diagnosis: identify blood loss, low intake, malabsorption, increased demand, or other cause.
  • Reticulocyte hemoglobin content (RetHb/RET-He) reflects iron available to newly produced RBCs and can detect functional deficiency earlier than mature-cell indices.
  • RetHb can help when ferritin is elevated by inflammation, but interpretation remains assay- and context-dependent.
  • Treatment corrects both the deficiency and its cause; oral or IV iron choice depends on severity, tolerance, absorption, ongoing loss, urgency, and comorbidity.

HIT Mechanism

  1. Activated platelets release PF4.
  2. Heparin binds PF4 and forms an immunogenic complex.
  3. IgG binds the complex and activates platelets through Fc-gamma-RIIa.
  4. Activated platelets release more PF4 and procoagulant material.
  5. Platelets are consumed, but thrombin generation creates a high thrombosis risk.

Paradox: thrombocytopenia plus thrombosis, not simply bleeding.

Course Quiz Anchors

  • Aplastic anemia = pancytopenia and infection/bleeding risk.
  • Megaloblastic anemia = defective DNA synthesis.
  • Pernicious anemia = B12 malabsorption from intrinsic-factor failure.
  • Reed-Sternberg cells = Hodgkin lymphoma.
  • ALL = children; AML = Auer rods; CML = Philadelphia chromosome; CLL = older adults.
  • Chronic inflammation = hepcidin-mediated iron restriction.

One-Page Cram Sheet

Fast Classifications

  • Macrocytic: B12/folate, marrow/drug/liver causes.
  • Microcytic: iron deficiency, inflammation, thalassemia, sideroblastic anemia.
  • Normocytic: blood loss, hemolysis, renal/inflammatory disease, aplasia.
  • HIT = thrombosis; ITP = isolated bleeding; TTP = MAHA + organ ischemia.
  • Vitamin K: II, VII, IX, X, proteins C/S.

Malignancy Anchors

  • Reed-Sternberg = Hodgkin lymphoma.
  • ALL = common in children.
  • AML = Auer rods in myeloid blasts.
  • CML = BCR-ABL1 t(9;22).
  • CLL = older adults, mature clonal B cells.

Practice and Course Quiz Review

1. How do lymphomas differ from leukemias?
Leukemias primarily arise in marrow and blood; lymphomas form masses in lymphoid tissues, though overlap and marrow involvement occur.
2. Why does aplastic anemia increase serious infection risk?
Stem-cell failure causes pancytopenia, including neutropenia as well as anemia and thrombocytopenia.
3. What causes megaloblastic morphology?
Defective DNA synthesis delays nuclear maturation, usually from B12 or folate deficiency.
4. What is the hallmark cell of Hodgkin lymphoma?
The Reed-Sternberg cell, a large abnormal B-lineage cell, classically binucleate or multinucleate.
5. What causes pernicious anemia?
Autoimmune intrinsic-factor/parietal-cell loss prevents vitamin B12 absorption.
6. What is the common adult cause of iron-deficiency anemia?
Chronic blood loss, commonly gastrointestinal or heavy menstrual bleeding; the source must be investigated.
7. Which leukemia is most common in children?
Acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL).
8. Where are Auer rods found?
In abnormal myeloid blasts or promyelocytes, supporting AML lineage.
9. What is the Philadelphia chromosome?
t(9;22) creates BCR-ABL1 tyrosine kinase, the defining molecular driver of CML.
10. How does inflammation cause anemia?
IL-6 raises hepcidin, which degrades ferroportin, traps iron in stores, reduces absorption, blunts EPO response, and shortens RBC survival.
11. What is the HIT paradox?
Immune platelet activation consumes platelets yet generates intense thrombin and thrombosis risk.
12. Compare HIT, ITP, and TTP.
HIT follows heparin and causes thrombosis; ITP causes isolated immune thrombocytopenic bleeding; TTP causes thrombocytopenia plus microangiopathic hemolysis and organ ischemia.
13. How does vitamin K deficiency impair coagulation?
It prevents gamma-carboxylation of factors II, VII, IX, X and proteins C/S, so they cannot bind calcium/phospholipid surfaces normally.
14. Why can liver disease cause both bleeding and thrombosis?
The liver produces both procoagulant and anticoagulant proteins; liver disease also alters platelets and fibrinolysis, creating fragile rebalanced hemostasis.
15. What distinguishes Hodgkin from non-Hodgkin lymphoma?
Hodgkin lymphoma features Reed-Sternberg cells and often orderly nodal spread; NHL is diverse and more often noncontiguous or extranodal.
16. Compare ALL, AML, CML, and CLL.
ALL is acute lymphoid and common in children; AML is acute myeloid with possible Auer rods; CML is BCR-ABL1-driven chronic myeloid disease; CLL is a chronic mature B-cell leukemia of older adults.
17. Why is RetHb useful?
It reflects hemoglobinization of newly produced reticulocytes and therefore recent functional iron availability, sometimes before mature RBC indices change.
18. Why does UFH inhibit thrombin more than LMWH?
UFH chains are long enough to bridge antithrombin and thrombin; shorter LMWH chains mainly accelerate factor Xa inhibition.
19. Why is HIT risk greater with UFH?
Longer UFH chains form larger, more immunogenic PF4-heparin complexes than LMWH.
20. Why does protamine only partly reverse LMWH?
Protamine neutralizes much of LMWH's anti-IIa activity but does not fully neutralize residual anti-Xa activity.
