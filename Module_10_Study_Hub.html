Normal Muscle and Bone Foundations

Muscle

Motor-neuron ACh triggers a muscle-fibre action potential. Sarcoplasmic-reticulum calcium exposes actin binding sites; ATP powers actinâ€“myosin cycling and calcium reuptake.

Bone Cells

Osteoblasts form osteoid and regulate mineralization; osteocytes sense load and maintain matrix; osteoclasts acidify and digest bone during resorption.

Mineral Control

PTH raises calcium through bone/renal/vitamin-D effects; calcitriol supports intestinal calcium/phosphate absorption; calcitonin inhibits osteoclast resorption.

Remodelling principleHealthy bone couples osteoclastic resorption to osteoblastic formation. Aging, endocrine disease, immobility, nutrition, medications, inflammation, and renal disease can shift the balance toward loss or defective mineralization.

1. Musculoskeletal Injuries

Injury Primary tissue/mechanism Typical manifestations
Strain Muscle or tendon overstretch/tear from excessive force or contraction. Pain with contraction/stretch, tenderness, spasm, swelling, weakness, bruising.
Sprain Ligament overstretch/tear, usually from joint force beyond physiologic range. Joint pain, swelling, bruising, reduced ROM; instability with major tears.
Dislocation Complete loss of normal articular contact, with capsule/ligament injury and possible neurovascular compromise. Deformity, severe pain, immobility, swelling; altered pulses/sensation are emergencies.
Subluxation Partial loss of joint congruence. Pain, abnormal alignment or movement, instability, reduced function.
Fracture Bone fails when applied load exceeds strength; may be traumatic, stress-related, or pathologic. Pain, swelling, deformity, crepitus, loss of function; open injury and neurovascular deficits raise urgency.

Fracture Healing

Hematoma/inflammationBleeding, cytokines, inflammatory-cell recruitment.
Soft callusGranulation tissue and fibrocartilage bridge the gap.
Hard callusWoven bone stabilizes the fracture.
RemodellingLamellar bone aligns with mechanical stress.
PEACE & LOVE frameworkEarly care: Protect, Elevate, Avoid anti-inflammatory modalities, Compress, Educate. Later care: Load, Optimism, Vascularisation, Exercise. Its central shift is from passive injury management toward education and progressive, symptom-guided loading.

2. Pain Pathways and Lower Back Pain

TransductionNoxious thermal, mechanical, or chemical stimuli activate nociceptors.
TransmissionA-delta and C fibres enter dorsal horn; ascending pathways reach thalamus, cortex, limbic system.
ModulationSpinal interneurons and descending brainstem pathways inhibit or facilitate signalling.
PerceptionSensory, emotional, cognitive, social, and contextual inputs create the pain experience.
Phenotype Mechanism Clinical clues
Nociceptive Actual/threatened non-neural tissue injury activates nociceptors. Proportionate, localized pain linked to movement/load or inflammation.
Neuropathic Lesion/disease of somatosensory nervous system. Burning/electric pain, paresthesia, allodynia, neuroanatomic distribution.
Nociplastic Altered nociception without sufficient tissue damage or somatosensory lesion to explain pain. Regional/multifocal pain, hypersensitivity, sleep/fatigue/cognitive symptoms; phenotype requires clinical assessment.

Peripheral Sensitization

Inflammatory mediators lower nociceptor thresholds at injured tissue, producing primary hyperalgesia and tenderness.

Central Sensitization

Amplified CNS signalling increases responsiveness to normal/subthreshold input, expanding pain fields and contributing to hyperalgesia, allodynia, and persistence.

Lower Back Pain

Pain may arise from discs, facet joints, vertebrae, ligaments, muscles, sacroiliac structures, or nerve roots. Degeneration can involve disc dehydration/height loss, annular fissuring, osteophytes, facet loading, stenosis, inflammation, and mechanical instability. Radicular pain reflects nerve-root irritation/compression; many cases remain nonspecific.

Red flagsMajor trauma, cancer/infection risk, fever, progressive motor deficit, saddle anesthesia, or new bladder/bowel dysfunction require urgent evaluation. Imaging findings and pain severity often correlate imperfectly.

7. Metabolic Joint Disease: Gout

HyperuricemiaUsually underexcretion; sometimes overproduction.
Crystal depositionMonosodium urate precipitates in cooler peripheral tissues.
InflammasomeMacrophage NLRP3 activation drives IL-1Î² release.
Acute arthritisNeutrophil recruitment produces severe pain, erythema, heat and swelling.
  • Classic first presentation is abrupt, intensely painful monoarthritis of the first MTP (podagra), but ankles, knees and other joints occur.
  • Chronic crystal burden can cause tophi, erosive arthritis, nephrolithiasis, and urate nephropathy.
  • Serum urate can be normal during a flare. Definitive diagnosis is needle-shaped, strongly negatively birefringent monosodium urate crystals in synovial fluid.

Clinical Consult Add-Ins

Vitamin D Deficiency and Bone Health

  • Vitamin D3 from skin or diet is converted to 25-hydroxyvitamin D in the liver, then activated to calcitriol in the kidney.
  • Deficiency reduces intestinal calcium absorption. Falling calcium stimulates PTH, which increases bone resorption and renal phosphate loss; limited calcium and phosphate impair mineralization.
  • Consequences include osteomalacia, fragility, proximal weakness and falls in adults, and rickets in growing children.
  • Higher-risk groups include older adults, people with limited sunlight, darker skin at northern latitudes, malabsorption, obesity, liver or kidney disease, and selected medications.
  • Serum 25(OH)D reflects vitamin D supply. Routine universal testing is unnecessary; test when risk, symptoms, or management decisions justify it.

Pain Care Without Stigma

ConceptMeaning
ToleranceNeuroadaptation reduces effect at the same dose.
Physical dependenceAdaptation produces withdrawal after abrupt cessation or antagonism; this alone is not addiction.
Opioid use disorderProblematic use with impaired control, craving, risky use, role consequences, and continued use despite harm.
  • Repeated mu-opioid receptor exposure changes signalling, stress systems, reward learning, and cue reactivity.
  • Use non-stigmatizing assessment, multimodal pain care, risk mitigation, naloxone education, and planned reassessment or tapering when appropriate.
  • OUD treatment includes buprenorphine/naloxone and methadone. Naloxone reverses acute toxicity but may wear off before the opioid.

OA vs RA at the Bedside

  • OA: gradual activity-related pain, brief morning stiffness, often asymmetric; cool hard bony enlargement, crepitus, Heberden nodes at DIP and Bouchard nodes at PIP.
  • RA: inflammatory pain and stiffness lasting more than 30-60 minutes; symmetric warm boggy MCP, PIP, and wrist synovitis; DIP joints usually spared.
  • Systemic fatigue, low-grade fever, weight change, and extra-articular disease favor RA.
  • OA radiographs show joint-space loss, osteophytes, subchondral sclerosis, and cysts. Ultrasound or MRI may detect earlier RA synovitis and erosive change.

RA Diagnostics and Treatment Logic

  • Anti-CCP is relatively specific; RF is less specific; ESR and CRP support inflammatory activity but may be normal.
  • Negative RF and anti-CCP do not exclude RA. A classic symmetric inflammatory pattern warrants timely rheumatology referral.
  • OA care targets symptoms, function, and mechanical load. RA requires early disease-modifying therapy to suppress immune activity and prevent pannus-driven erosion.
  • Analgesics or NSAIDs alone may reduce RA symptoms but do not prevent structural damage.

Practice and Active Recall

1. What tissue distinguishes a strain from a sprain?
A strain injures muscle or tendon; a sprain injures ligament.
2. What separates dislocation from subluxation?
Dislocation is complete loss of articular contact; subluxation is partial loss of congruence.
3. Name the four broad fracture-healing stages.
Hematoma/inflammation, soft callus, hard bony callus, and remodelling.
4. What does PEACE & LOVE emphasize?
Early protection, elevation, avoidance of unnecessary anti-inflammatory modalities, compression and education, followed by progressive loading, optimism, vascular activity and exercise.
5. Trace nociception from tissue to perception.
Transduction at nociceptors â†’ transmission through A-delta/C fibres and spinal pathways â†’ modulation in spinal/descending systems â†’ cortical/limbic perception shaped by context.
6. Peripheral versus central sensitization?
Peripheral sensitization lowers thresholds at injured tissue; central sensitization amplifies CNS responsiveness and can broaden/persist pain beyond tissue healing.
7. What makes pain biopsychosocial?
Sensory input is interpreted alongside attention, expectation, emotion, past experience, sleep, stress, culture, relationships, work and social safety.
8. What is nociplastic pain?
Pain arising from altered nociception without clear evidence of sufficient tissue damage or a somatosensory lesion to explain it.
9. Osteoporosis versus osteomalacia?
Osteoporosis is reduced quantity/microarchitectural strength of normally mineralized bone; osteomalacia is defective mineralization of osteoid.
10. What do T-score and Z-score compare?
T-score compares BMD with a young-adult reference; Z-score compares with age- and sex-matched peers.
11. How does vitamin D deficiency damage bone?
Reduced calcium/phosphate absorption impairs mineralization and can provoke secondary hyperparathyroidism, worsening skeletal mineral loss.
12. What is the most common osteomyelitis organism?
Staphylococcus aureus, though age, exposure, wound and host factors change the differential.
13. Define sequestrum and involucrum.
A sequestrum is devitalized infected bone; an involucrum is reactive new bone forming around it.
14. What features suggest malignant rather than benign bone tumour?
Progressive pain/mass, wide transition zone, cortical destruction, aggressive periosteal reaction, soft-tissue extension and metastatic potential.
15. What is the core OA mechanism?
Whole-joint degeneration involving cartilage matrix loss, subchondral remodelling, osteophytes and variable synovial inflammation.
16. What is the core RA mechanism?
Autoimmune synovitis driven by immune cells and cytokines forms pannus, activates osteoclasts and destroys cartilage and bone.
17. Which biomarkers are high-yield in RA?
Anti-CCP is relatively specific; rheumatoid factor supports diagnosis but is less specific; ESR and CRP reflect inflammatory activity but are nonspecific.
18. How do TNF, IL-6, IL-1 and RANKL contribute to RA?
They sustain synovitis, systemic inflammation, cartilage-degrading enzymes and osteoclast activation leading to erosions.
19. What triggers an acute gout flare?
Monosodium urate crystals activate the NLRP3 inflammasome and IL-1Î², recruiting neutrophils into the joint.
20. What synovial-fluid finding confirms gout?
Needle-shaped, strongly negatively birefringent monosodium urate crystals.
21. How does vitamin D deficiency weaken bone?
Reduced calcium absorption triggers secondary hyperparathyroidism and phosphate loss, increases resorption, and leaves inadequate mineral supply for normal osteoid mineralization.
22. Why is serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D used to assess vitamin D status?
It is the major circulating storage form and reflects vitamin D supply better than tightly regulated active calcitriol.
23. How do opioid tolerance, physical dependence, and OUD differ?
Tolerance is reduced response at the same dose; dependence is withdrawal after abrupt cessation; OUD is impaired control, craving, risky use, and continued use despite harm.
24. Why can naloxone reversal require repeated dosing and observation?
Naloxone may wear off before the causative opioid, allowing respiratory depression to recur.
25. Which hand-joint pattern favors RA over OA?
Symmetric warm or boggy MCP, PIP, and wrist synovitis with prolonged morning stiffness and DIP sparing favors RA.
26. Do negative RF and anti-CCP results rule out RA?
No. Seronegative RA exists; diagnosis and referral still depend on the inflammatory history, examination pattern, and imaging.
27. Why are analgesics alone inadequate for RA?
They may reduce pain but do not suppress autoimmune synovitis, pannus formation, osteoclast activation, or progressive erosive damage; DMARD therapy is required.
