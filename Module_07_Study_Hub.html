Endocrine Disorder Framework

  • Endocrine tissues release hormones into blood, where they bind receptors on distant target cells.
  • Disorders are categorized by affected organ, direction of change, and mechanism.
  • Direction: hyperfunction versus hypofunction.
  • Mechanism: primary disease directly affects the endocrine gland; secondary disease changes upstream hypothalamic or pituitary signaling.

Hormone Feedback Loops

  • Hypothalamic releasing hormones stimulate anterior pituitary hormones, which stimulate target endocrine glands.
  • Target gland hormones usually provide negative feedback to the pituitary and hypothalamus.
  • High target hormone with low pituitary signal often suggests primary hyperfunction.
  • Low target hormone with high pituitary signal often suggests primary hypofunction.

Diabetes Diagnosis Criteria From Slides

  • Fasting plasma glucose 7.0 mmol/L or higher.
  • A1C 6.5% or higher in adults when assay conditions are valid and type 1 diabetes is not suspected.
  • 2-hour plasma glucose 11.1 mmol/L or higher after 75 g OGTT.
  • Random plasma glucose 11.1 mmol/L or higher in the right clinical context.

Exercise Physiology and Type 2 DM

  • Aerobic exercise increases glucose uptake through GLUT4, initially in an insulin-independent way.
  • Insulin sensitivity can remain increased for 2 to 72 hours after aerobic exercise.
  • Aerobic training improves hepatic glucose output regulation, lipid profile, adipokine activity, blood pressure, and mood.
  • Resistance training increases muscle mass and improves fasting glucose and insulin action.
  • Combined aerobic and resistance exercise is most effective for glucose control and weight management.

Endocrine Control Questions

  • The pituitary is traditionally called the master endocrine gland, while the hypothalamus controls it through releasing and inhibiting hormones.
  • Hormone release can be triggered by humoral stimuli, hormonal stimuli, or neural stimuli.
  • Anterior pituitary synthesizes and secretes ACTH, TSH, FSH, LH, growth hormone, and prolactin.
  • Posterior pituitary stores and releases ADH and oxytocin that are synthesized in hypothalamic neurons.
  • Endocrine disease can reflect hormone excess, hormone deficiency, abnormal receptors, altered intracellular response, feedback failure, or ectopic hormone production.

Feedback Loop Language

  • Ultra-short feedback: hormone acts back on the secreting cell or target organ itself.
  • Short feedback: pituitary tropic hormones feed back to the hypothalamus or pituitary level.
  • Long feedback: target gland hormones travel back to inhibit hypothalamus and pituitary signaling.
  • When reviewing endocrine pathology, ask whether the feedback loop is intact or disrupted because this determines labs, manifestations, and treatment logic.

1. Disorders of the Pituitary Gland

Primary Pituitary Adenoma

  • Usually benign monoclonal tumors of anterior pituitary cells.
  • Can be functioning, secreting excess hormone, or nonfunctioning, causing mass effect.
  • Mass effect can compress the optic chiasm, causing bitemporal visual field loss, headache, and hypopituitarism.
  • Clinical findings depend on hormone type: prolactin, growth hormone, ACTH, TSH, or gonadotropin effects.

Acromegaly

  • Most often caused by a growth hormone secreting pituitary adenoma after epiphyseal closure.
  • Growth hormone stimulates hepatic IGF-1, driving soft tissue, cartilage, bone, and organ growth.
  • Manifestations: enlarged hands/feet, coarse facial features, jaw enlargement, spacing of teeth, thickened skin, sweating, arthralgia, headaches, visual defects.
  • Metabolic effects include insulin resistance, hypertension, cardiomyopathy, sleep apnea, and increased cardiovascular risk.

2. Disorders of the Thyroid Gland

Hyperthyroidism and Graves Disease

  • Hyperthyroidism is excess thyroid hormone effect, increasing basal metabolic rate and sympathetic responsiveness.
  • Graves disease is autoimmune hyperthyroidism caused by thyroid-stimulating immunoglobulins activating the TSH receptor.
  • Common manifestations: weight loss despite appetite, heat intolerance, sweating, tremor, anxiety, palpitations, tachycardia, diarrhea, menstrual changes, goiter.
  • Graves-specific clues can include ophthalmopathy, lid lag, exophthalmos, and pretibial myxedema.

Thyrotoxic Crisis

  • Severe, life-threatening exaggeration of thyrotoxicosis, often triggered by infection, trauma, surgery, childbirth, MI, or stopping antithyroid therapy.
  • Manifestations: high fever, severe tachycardia, arrhythmia, heart failure, agitation, delirium, vomiting, diarrhea, dehydration, and shock risk.
  • Clinical priority is recognizing endocrine emergency physiology: extreme metabolic demand plus cardiovascular strain.

Addison Transcript Details

  • Addison disease is primary adrenal insufficiency from adrenal cortex damage, most commonly autoimmune autoreactive T-cell destruction.
  • Other causes include tuberculosis, HIV, adrenal hemorrhage, and trauma.
  • Autoimmune Addison disease often targets steroidogenic enzyme 21-hydroxylase; 21-hydroxylase antibodies are a common lab clue.
  • Clinical manifestations may not appear until about 90% of adrenocortical cells are damaged.
  • Adrenal cortex hormone layers: zona glomerulosa makes aldosterone, zona fasciculata makes cortisol, zona reticularis makes adrenal androgens.

Addison Crisis Logic

  • Aldosterone loss lowers sodium reabsorption and potassium excretion, causing hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, salt craving, dehydration, and orthostatic hypotension.
  • Cortisol loss removes negative feedback, raising ACTH. ACTH and melanocyte-stimulating hormone share a precursor, which contributes to hyperpigmentation.
  • Low cortisol increases ADH activity and can worsen hyponatremia.
  • Adrenal crisis is often triggered by infection, procedure, trauma, or severe psychological stress.
  • Crisis clues include refractory hypotension, hypoglycemia, hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, GI symptoms, altered level of consciousness, syncope, and shock risk.

Prediabetes and Lifestyle

  • Prediabetes includes impaired fasting glucose, impaired glucose tolerance, or A1C 6.0% to 6.4% in the Diabetes Canada framing used by the slides.
  • Reducing highly processed foods, refined grains, sugary foods, and sugary drinks helps lower risk.
  • DASH-style or Mediterranean-style eating patterns are highlighted as practical prevention approaches.
  • Interrupting sitting every 20 to 30 minutes and aiming for at least 150 minutes/week of aerobic exercise supports glucose homeostasis.
  • Losing about 5% of initial body weight can delay or prevent type 2 diabetes in people with prediabetes.

Clinical Practice Quick Cards

Endocrine Pattern Recognition

  • Ask whether the disorder is hormone excess, hormone deficiency, receptor stimulation/resistance, or gland destruction.
  • Separate primary gland disease from secondary pituitary/hypothalamic disease using feedback logic.
  • Look for emergency physiology: thyroid storm, adrenal crisis, DKA, HHS, severe hypoglycemia, severe hyponatremia.

Diabetes Red Flags

  • Vomiting, abdominal pain, Kussmaul respirations, fruity breath, or altered mental status in hyperglycemia.
  • Severe dehydration, neurologic symptoms, and extreme hyperglycemia in older type 2 diabetes patients.
  • Hypoglycemia with confusion, seizure, or inability to self-treat.

Electrolyte Clues

  • DI: water loss, dilute urine, hypernatremia risk.
  • SIADH: water retention, concentrated urine, hyponatremia risk.
  • Addison disease: hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, hypotension.
  • Parathyroid disorders: calcium-driven neuromuscular, renal, bone, and GI patterns.
Transcript-derived Hashimoto consult notesHashimoto thyroiditis is the most common hypothyroidism cause in iodine-sufficient regions. It is more common in females, often begins between ages 30-50, clusters in families, and is associated with other autoimmune diseases such as type 1 diabetes and celiac disease.

Hashimoto Mechanism and Labs

  • Risk factors converge on altered thyroid antigen presentation and loss of tolerance to TPO and thyroglobulin.
  • Th1 cells, CD8 cytotoxicity, macrophage inflammation, anti-TPO/anti-TG antibodies, complement, and antibody-dependent cell-mediated cytotoxicity all contribute to follicular injury.
  • Early inflammation plus TSH stimulation can cause goiter; chronic fibrosis can make the gland small and firm.
  • TSH is the best initial screen; free T4 distinguishes overt from subclinical disease when TSH is abnormal.
  • Anti-TPO is the most useful autoimmune marker; anti-TG is supportive but less sensitive.

Levothyroxine Monitoring

  • Levothyroxine is preferred because T4 is converted peripherally to T3 and gives stable once-daily replacement.
  • Typical replacement target discussed: about 1.6 mcg/kg/day, with lower starts in older or cardiac patients.
  • Check TSH 6-8 weeks after starting or changing dose, then every 6-12 months once stable.
  • Separate levothyroxine from iron, calcium, antacids, PPIs, soy, and other interfering products; the transcript emphasized about 4 hours separation for interacting medications.
  • Over-replacement warning signs: palpitations, chest pain, tremor, unexplained weight loss; risks include atrial fibrillation, coronary disease, heart failure, osteoporosis, and fractures.
  • Pregnancy usually requires a 20-30% levothyroxine increase when pregnancy is confirmed, closer TSH monitoring, and reduction back toward baseline around 6-8 weeks postpartum.
  • Unregulated high-dose iodine can worsen autoimmune thyroiditis and should not replace levothyroxine.

Addison Clinical Consult

  • Addison disease is chronic primary adrenal insufficiency from adrenal cortex damage or dysfunction, most commonly autoimmune destruction of adrenocortical cells.
  • Core hormone losses are cortisol and aldosterone, with increased ACTH from loss of negative feedback and impaired stress homeostasis.
  • Adrenal cortex memory aid: zona glomerulosa makes aldosterone for salt and water balance; zona fasciculata makes cortisol for sugar, stress, vascular tone, and immune effects; zona reticularis makes adrenal androgen precursors.
  • Common manifestations include fatigue, muscle weakness, weight loss, anorexia, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, orthostatic hypotension, hyperpigmentation or vitiligo, decreased libido, and loss of axillary or pubic hair.
  • Adrenal crisis is systemic deterioration with rapid shock risk, often after infection, trauma, procedure, exercise stress, or psychological stress.
  • Crisis cues include severe dehydration, refractory hypotension, altered level of consciousness, syncope, hypoglycemia, hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, abdominal pain, vomiting, diarrhea, and low back pain.

Addison Diagnosis and Management

  • Diagnostic workup combines clinical findings with morning serum cortisol, plasma ACTH, 21-hydroxylase antibodies for autoimmune etiology, and ACTH stimulation testing as the diagnostic gold standard.
  • Primary adrenal insufficiency pattern: low cortisol, high ACTH, hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, hypoglycemia, decreased DHEA-S, and sometimes elevated TSH.
  • Treatment includes glucocorticoid replacement such as hydrocortisone or prednisone plus mineralocorticoid replacement with fludrocortisone when aldosterone is deficient.
  • Stress dosing is required during illness or physiologic stress to mimic the normal cortisol stress response.
  • Adrenal crisis treatment should not wait for lab confirmation: give IV hydrocortisone and rapid IV fluid resuscitation.
  • Low-sodium dieting is contraindicated because aldosterone loss already causes renal sodium wasting, hyponatremia, hypovolemia, and hypotension risk.

Hashimoto Consult Presentation

  • Hashimoto thyroiditis is the leading hypothyroidism cause in iodine-sufficient regions such as North America.
  • Risk is higher in females, often peaks around ages 30-50, clusters in families, and is associated with autoimmune diseases such as type 1 diabetes, celiac disease, pernicious anemia, rheumatoid arthritis, and vitiligo.
  • Excess iodine can precipitate or worsen autoimmune thyroiditis, while iodine deficiency impairs thyroid hormone production and changes disease patterns.
  • Typical presentation includes fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, constipation, slowed cognition or depression, dry skin, hair loss, brittle nails, myalgias, puffy face, nonpitting edema, bradycardia, delayed reflexes, and painless goiter early.
  • Complications include hyperlipidemia, accelerated atherosclerosis, reduced cardiac output, pericardial effusion, menstrual irregularities, infertility, pregnancy risks, rare myxedema coma, and rare thyroid lymphoma with long-standing disease.

Hashimoto Treatment Safety

  • Levothyroxine is first-line for overt hypothyroidism; consider treatment in subclinical disease when symptomatic, pregnant, or high cardiovascular risk.
  • Use a consistent formulation when possible because brand/formulation changes can alter TSH control.
  • Recheck TSH about 4-8 weeks after starting or changing therapy, then continue monitoring until stable.
  • Synthetic T4 is preferred because it has stable bioavailability, a long half-life, peripheral conversion to T3, predictable pharmacodynamics, and fewer peaks or troughs than T3 therapy.
  • Avoid over-the-counter thyroid supplements, high-dose iodine, and routine selenium use unless specifically indicated.
  • New palpitations, chest pain, tremor, or unexplained weight loss on levothyroxine should prompt evaluation for iatrogenic thyrotoxicosis.

Practice and Active Recall

1. What is the main difference between a functioning and nonfunctioning pituitary adenoma?
A functioning adenoma secretes excess hormone; a nonfunctioning adenoma mainly causes mass effect or hypopituitarism.
2. Why does acromegaly occur after epiphyseal closure?
Growth hormone excess after growth plates close cannot increase height, so it causes soft tissue, bone, cartilage, and organ enlargement.
3. What is the fastest DI versus SIADH contrast?
DI is too little ADH effect causing water loss, dilute urine, polyuria, and hypernatremia risk. SIADH is too much ADH effect causing water retention, concentrated urine, and hyponatremia risk.
4. What drives Graves disease?
Autoantibodies stimulate the TSH receptor, causing excess thyroid hormone production and thyroid growth.
5. Why can iodine deficiency cause goiter?
Low iodine lowers T3/T4 synthesis, increasing TSH through loss of negative feedback. TSH stimulates thyroid growth.
6. What is Hashimoto disease?
Autoimmune thyroiditis that progressively destroys thyroid tissue, causing primary hypothyroidism.
7. Hyperparathyroidism versus hypoparathyroidism: what calcium pattern do you expect?
Hyperparathyroidism usually causes hypercalcemia; hypoparathyroidism causes hypocalcemia.
8. What causes type 1 diabetes?
Autoimmune destruction of pancreatic beta cells, causing absolute insulin deficiency.
9. What causes type 2 diabetes?
Insulin resistance plus progressive beta-cell dysfunction, causing relative insulin deficiency and chronic hyperglycemia.
10. How does metabolic syndrome connect to type 2 diabetes?
Central adiposity and insulin resistance increase beta-cell demand. Over time, beta-cell compensation fails and hyperglycemia develops.
11. Why does DKA produce metabolic acidosis?
Insulin deficiency promotes lipolysis and hepatic ketone production. Ketone accumulation lowers blood pH.
12. Why is HHS usually less ketotic than DKA?
There is usually enough insulin activity to suppress major ketogenesis, but not enough to prevent severe hyperglycemia and dehydration.
13. What are the three major diabetic microvascular complications?
Retinopathy, nephropathy, and neuropathy.
14. Why does diabetes increase infection risk?
Hyperglycemia impairs immune cell function, circulation, wound healing, and tissue defense.
15. What is the difference between Cushing disease and Cushing syndrome?
Cushing disease is pituitary ACTH excess causing cortisol excess. Cushing syndrome is the broader state of cortisol excess from any cause.
16. Why does Addison disease cause hyperpigmentation?
Primary adrenal failure lowers cortisol, raising ACTH. ACTH-related melanocortin activity increases skin pigmentation.
17. What electrolyte pattern is high-yield for Addison disease?
Hyponatremia and hyperkalemia from aldosterone deficiency, often with hypotension and dehydration.
18. What makes thyrotoxic crisis dangerous?
Extreme thyroid hormone effect creates severe metabolic demand and cardiovascular strain, causing fever, tachyarrhythmias, heart failure, delirium, dehydration, and shock risk.
19. What causes myxedema in hypothyroidism?
Proteins and mucopolysaccharides accumulate in the dermis, bind water, and cause nonpitting cutaneous edema such as puffy face, periorbital edema, or pretibial edema.
20. Which tutorial answer best describes SIADH?
SIADH involves increased renal water retention, hypo-osmolality, and hyponatremia from dilution rather than true sodium deficiency.
21. What is the incorrect metabolic syndrome risk factor from the tutorial list?
Low triglyceride level is not a risk factor. Metabolic syndrome includes high triglycerides, low HDL, hypertension, abdominal obesity, insulin resistance, and elevated glucose.
22. What causes diabetic microvascular complications?
Capillary basement membrane thickening, endothelial hyperplasia, thrombosis, glycation, oxidative stress, and hyperglycemia-related tissue injury drive microvascular disease.
23. What is the mechanism of metformin emphasized in the tutorial?
Metformin lowers high blood glucose primarily by suppressing hepatic gluconeogenesis and reducing excessive hepatic glucose output.
24. What lifestyle effect can delay type 2 diabetes in prediabetes?
A nutrition and exercise plan that produces about 5% initial body weight loss can delay or prevent progression to type 2 diabetes.
25. What normally triggers posterior pituitary ADH release?
Increased plasma osmolarity is the key normal trigger; falling blood pressure or volume can also stimulate ADH.
26. What is the key lab contrast between DI and SIADH?
DI tends toward high serum osmolarity, hypernatremia risk, high urine output, and dilute urine. SIADH tends toward low serum sodium/osmolarity with concentrated urine and higher urine sodium.
27. Why can ACTH deficiency be life-threatening?
ACTH deficiency can cause secondary adrenal insufficiency with cortisol deficiency, hypoglycemia, hypotension, and adrenal crisis risk.
28. What stimulates PTH release?
Low ionized calcium decreases calcium-sensing receptor activation on parathyroid chief cells, stimulating PTH release.
29. What is pseudohypoparathyroidism?
It is tissue resistance to PTH, causing hypocalcemia despite normal or elevated PTH levels.
30. Which Hashimoto antibody is most useful clinically?
Anti-TPO antibodies are the most useful marker of autoimmune thyroiditis and help predict progression from subclinical to overt hypothyroidism.
31. Why can Addison disease cause hyperpigmentation?
Low cortisol removes negative feedback, raising ACTH. ACTH and melanocyte-stimulating hormone share a precursor, increasing melanocyte stimulation and melanin production.
32. What are the adrenal cortex layers and hormone groups?
Zona glomerulosa produces aldosterone for salt/water balance, zona fasciculata produces cortisol for stress/glucose/vascular tone, and zona reticularis produces adrenal androgens.
33. A patient with known Addison disease has fever but is stable and tolerating oral intake. What teaching is most important?
Increase glucocorticoid dosing for illness/stress according to the prescribed sick-day plan instead of stopping, reducing, or simply continuing the usual dose without adjustment.
34. Which lab pattern supports primary adrenal insufficiency?
Increased ACTH with decreased cortisol, hyponatremia, and hyperkalemia supports primary adrenal insufficiency.
35. Why should a patient with Addison disease avoid a low-sodium diet?
Aldosterone deficiency causes renal sodium wasting, so sodium restriction can worsen hyponatremia, hypovolemia, and hypotension.
36. What is the first intervention for suspected adrenal crisis with vomiting, hypotension, and altered mental status?
Give IV hydrocortisone and begin rapid IV fluid resuscitation. Do not wait for lab confirmation before treating suspected adrenal crisis.
37. Which immune mechanism best explains Hashimoto thyroid injury?
Th1-mediated cell-mediated cytotoxicity, with CD8 cytotoxic injury, macrophage inflammation, and anti-TPO/anti-thyroglobulin antibodies contributing to follicular damage.
38. Which risk factor is recognized for Hashimoto thyroiditis?
Personal or family history of autoimmune disease is a recognized risk factor, including type 1 diabetes, celiac disease, pernicious anemia, rheumatoid arthritis, and vitiligo.
39. Why is levothyroxine preferred over liothyronine for routine hypothyroidism replacement?
T4 is converted peripherally to T3 and provides steadier once-daily replacement with a longer half-life and fewer peaks or troughs.
40. What symptom pattern should trigger evaluation for levothyroxine over-replacement?
New palpitations, chest pain, tremor, or unexplained weight loss can signal iatrogenic thyrotoxicosis and should be evaluated promptly.
