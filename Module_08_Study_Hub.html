Diagnostic Framing

Why Diagnostic Systems Matter

  • DSM and ICD systems create shared language for clinicians, researchers, patients, families, services, statistics, and treatment planning.
  • Rule-based criteria improve diagnostic agreement, but categories are still clinical constructs rather than perfectly bounded biological entities.
  • Pathophysiology matters because mental illnesses are syndromes with overlapping symptoms, genetic risk, neurobiology, and environmental triggers.

How to Study This Module

  • Do not memorize diagnoses as isolated labels. Connect symptoms to stress systems, neurotransmitters, brain circuits, reward learning, and threat processing.
  • For each disorder, ask: What is the dominant circuit? What neurotransmitters are implicated? What clinical manifestations follow?
  • Separate pathophysiology from diagnostic criteria. The exam objective is mechanism plus manifestations.

1. Stress Response

Stress perceptionThreat, uncertainty, trauma, pain, illness, or psychosocial strain activates central stress appraisal.
->
SNS/SAM systemSympathetic activation and adrenal medulla catecholamines increase heart rate, blood pressure, bronchodilation, glucose availability, and vigilance.
->
HPA axisHypothalamic CRH stimulates pituitary ACTH, which stimulates adrenal cortisol release.
->
Allostatic loadRepeated or prolonged activation can dysregulate immune, metabolic, sleep, mood, and cognitive systems.

Acute Stress: Adaptive

  • SNS activation mobilizes rapid fight-or-flight physiology through norepinephrine and epinephrine.
  • Cortisol supports glucose availability, vascular responsiveness, attention, and energy mobilization.
  • Negative feedback normally reduces CRH and ACTH after the stressor resolves.

Chronic Stress: Pathologic

  • Chronic stress can keep cortisol and catecholamine signaling activated or dysregulated.
  • Persistent activation can alter sleep, reward, attention, fear conditioning, inflammation, autonomic tone, and neuroplasticity.
  • Stress biology can increase vulnerability to depression, anxiety, PTSD, substance use relapse, psychosis vulnerability, and disordered eating patterns.

2. Schizophrenia Spectrum

Symptom groupClinical manifestationsStudy cue
Positive symptomsHallucinations, delusions, disorganized speech, disorganized behavior, agitation, suspiciousness.Added experiences or distortions of reality.
Negative symptomsFlat affect, alogia, avolition, anhedonia, asociality, reduced motivation, social withdrawal.Loss or reduction of normal emotional, social, or goal-directed function.
Cognitive symptomsImpaired attention, working memory, executive function, processing speed, learning, and social cognition.Often drives functional impairment even when positive symptoms improve.

Neurotransmitter Signaling

  • Dopamine dysregulation is central: increased mesolimbic dopamine is linked with positive symptoms.
  • Reduced mesocortical dopamine signaling is linked with negative and cognitive symptoms.
  • Glutamate/NMDA receptor hypofunction and GABA interneuron dysfunction may disrupt cortical coordination and cognition.

Brain Circuit Changes

  • Schizophrenia is often framed as a neurodevelopmental disorder with genetic vulnerability plus environmental risk.
  • Findings may involve prefrontal cortex, hippocampus, temporal regions, thalamic filtering, and connectivity networks.
  • Clinical pattern reflects disrupted salience, perception, working memory, motivation, and reality testing.

4. Substance Use Disorders

Risk Factors

  • Genetic vulnerability, early substance exposure, trauma, chronic stress, psychiatric comorbidity, impulsivity, peer use, family history, social adversity, and high availability increase risk.
  • Adolescents are vulnerable because reward and learning systems mature earlier than executive control systems.
  • Protective factors include stable support, delayed exposure, mental health treatment, low availability, and skills for coping with stress and peer pressure.

Neurobiology

  • Addictive substances strongly activate mesolimbic reward pathways and dopamine signaling, reinforcing use.
  • Repeated use shifts behavior from reward-driven use toward habit, craving, withdrawal avoidance, and impaired control.
  • Stress systems and negative affect become linked to relapse as tolerance, withdrawal, cue reactivity, and compulsive seeking develop.
Clinical domainManifestationsPathophysiology link
Impaired controlUsing more than intended, unsuccessful cut-down attempts, craving, time spent obtaining/using/recovering.Reward learning, cue reactivity, executive control impairment.
Social impairmentRole failure, interpersonal conflict, reduced activities.Substance priority increases while alternative rewards lose salience.
Risky useUse in hazardous situations or despite physical/psychological harm.Threat appraisal and impulse control are outweighed by reward/withdrawal circuits.
Pharmacologic adaptationTolerance and withdrawal.Neuroadaptation maintains homeostasis in the presence of repeated exposure.

Clinical consult add-inThe Eating Disorders presentation reinforces that binge eating is not the same as overeating and adds prevalence, sex/gender minority risk, visible clinical clues, and treatment options.

Eating Disorder Risk Factors

  • Risk factors include genetics, personality traits, parental teasing about weight, neglect, abuse, trauma, sociocultural ideals, media pressure, and mental health comorbidity.
  • Perfectionism and obsession were linked with anorexia nervosa restricting type; impulsiveness was linked with anorexia binge/purge type, bulimia nervosa, and binge eating disorder.
  • Sexual and gender minority people have increased eating disorder risk, framed through minority stress: stigma, bullying, discrimination, low support, mental health comorbidity, and lack of inclusive health care.

Clinical Clues and Treatment

  • Russell's sign refers to calluses or injury on the knuckles or back of the hand from self-induced vomiting.
  • Parotid gland enlargement can appear in anorexia binge/purge type or bulimia nervosa because of recurrent purging.
  • Wearing loose clothing or multiple layers to hide body shape can be seen with anorexia nervosa and other body-image driven eating disorders.
  • Treatment options from the presentation include family-based treatment, CBT, guided self-help, focal psychodynamic therapy, fluoxetine for bulimia or binge eating disorder, olanzapine for anorexia nervosa, and lisdexamfetamine for binge eating disorder.

PTSD clinical consult add-inThe PTSD presentation adds pandemic-related at-risk groups, dissociative specifiers, corticolimbic dysfunction, HPA-axis dysregulation, inflammation, neurotransmitter imbalance, and treatment planning.

PTSD Risk and Diagnosis Details

  • Risk can be pretraumatic, peritraumatic, or posttraumatic: prior mental health conditions, childhood adversity, low socioeconomic status or support, genetic/epigenetic vulnerability, trauma intensity, perceived threat, physical injury, dissociation, ongoing stressors, discrimination, and poor coping.
  • DSM-5 symptom clusters include intrusion, avoidance, negative alterations in cognition and mood, and marked alterations in arousal and reactivity.
  • Specify dissociative symptoms when depersonalization or derealization is present; delayed onset means symptoms fully emerge at least 6 months after trauma.
  • Children age 6 or younger may show trauma-related play, distressing dreams without clear content, and less detailed trauma recall.

PTSD Mechanism to Manifestations

  • Reduced hippocampal function impairs fear extinction and makes it harder to distinguish past trauma from present safety.
  • Decreased medial prefrontal cortex and anterior cingulate activity reduce top-down control over limbic regions.
  • Amygdala hyperreactivity increases threat perception, sustained fear, and stress responses.
  • Often reduced cortisol output despite ongoing stress activation can weaken anti-inflammatory control, while chronic sympathetic activation raises inflammatory cytokines such as IL-6, IL-1, and TNF-alpha.
  • Noradrenergic and orexin activity contribute to hyperarousal, hypervigilance, and sleep disturbance; glutamate-GABA imbalance can strengthen fear circuits and intrusive memories.

Fast Condition Links

  • Stress response is the foundation: SNS gives rapid arousal; HPA axis gives cortisol-mediated adaptation.
  • Depression and PTSD both connect to stress biology, but PTSD centers on trauma-linked fear learning and intrusion/avoidance.
  • Panic disorder and PTSD both involve autonomic arousal, but panic is recurrent fear of bodily surges while PTSD is anchored to trauma exposure and reminders.
  • OCD and anxiety share distress, but OCD is driven by obsessions and compulsions reinforced by temporary relief.

Neurotransmitter Links

  • Dopamine: reward learning, mania risk, psychosis/salience, substance reinforcement.
  • Norepinephrine: vigilance, panic symptoms, PTSD hyperarousal, withdrawal arousal.
  • Serotonin: mood, anxiety regulation, obsessive symptoms, appetite/satiety links.
  • Glutamate/GABA: excitation-inhibition balance, fear circuits, cognition, schizophrenia and PTSD mechanisms.

Clinical Consult Add-Ins

Eating Disorders Consult

  • Anorexia nervosa includes restricting type and binge eating/purging type; signs may include low BMI, intense preoccupation with food, weight loss, abdominal pain, dizziness, and parotid gland enlargement.
  • Bulimia nervosa includes recurrent dietary restriction and binge eating with compensatory behaviors such as purging; clues include Russell's sign, dehydration, weight change, and parotid enlargement.
  • Binge eating disorder involves recurring binge eating without regular compensatory behaviors; it is distinct from ordinary overeating and may involve food anxiety, shame, weight change, and body-shape concealment.
  • Prevalence is higher in girls and young women, but eating disorders also occur in boys, men, and sexual/gender minority populations.

PTSD Consult

  • PTSD pathophysiology links trauma exposure to HPA-axis dysregulation, corticolimbic dysfunction, chronic inflammation, and neurotransmitter imbalance.
  • Brain pattern: reduced hippocampal function, decreased medial prefrontal/anterior cingulate control, and increased amygdala reactivity.
  • Clinical symptom clusters are intrusion, avoidance, negative mood/cognition alterations, and arousal/reactivity changes, with dissociation in some patients.
  • Care planning should be collaborative, recovery-goal focused, trauma-informed, and attentive to social determinants of health.
  • Treatment options noted include trauma-informed psychotherapy, CBT, DBT, prolonged exposure, cognitive processing therapy, EMDR, SSRIs/SNRIs, prazosin for nightmares with blood pressure monitoring, and psychiatry referral for complexity or treatment resistance.

Practice and Active Recall

1. What are the two major physiologic arms of the stress response?
The sympathetic nervous system/adrenal medulla response provides rapid catecholamine-driven fight-or-flight effects. The HPA axis provides CRH to ACTH to cortisol signaling for longer energy mobilization and stress adaptation.
2. How can chronic stress contribute to mental illness?
Chronic stress can dysregulate cortisol, autonomic tone, sleep, immune signaling, reward pathways, fear conditioning, and neuroplasticity, increasing vulnerability to depression, anxiety, PTSD, substance use relapse, and other psychiatric symptoms.
3. What is the difference between positive and negative schizophrenia symptoms?
Positive symptoms are added experiences such as hallucinations, delusions, and disorganized speech. Negative symptoms are reductions in normal function such as flat affect, avolition, alogia, anhedonia, and social withdrawal.
4. Which schizophrenia symptoms often drive functional impairment?
Cognitive symptoms such as impaired attention, working memory, executive function, processing speed, and social cognition often drive long-term functional impairment.
5. What dopamine pattern is high-yield for schizophrenia?
Increased mesolimbic dopamine is linked with positive symptoms, while reduced mesocortical dopamine signaling is linked with negative and cognitive symptoms.
6. What separates major depression from bipolar disorder?
Bipolar disorder includes mania or hypomania. Major depression does not include a history of mania or hypomania.
7. What clinical manifestations suggest mania?
Elevated or irritable mood, increased energy, decreased need for sleep, pressured speech, racing thoughts, impulsivity, grandiosity, distractibility, and risky behavior.
8. What is the core reward pathway issue in substance use disorders?
Substances strongly activate reward and dopamine learning pathways, then repeated exposure produces neuroadaptation, craving, impaired control, tolerance, withdrawal, and relapse vulnerability.
9. Why are adolescents at particular risk for substance use disorders?
Reward and learning systems are highly responsive while executive control systems are still maturing, making early exposure, peer influence, stress, and impulsivity more impactful.
10. What distinguishes anorexia nervosa, bulimia nervosa, and binge eating disorder?
Anorexia centers on restriction and significantly low weight. Bulimia involves binge eating plus compensatory behaviors. Binge eating disorder involves recurrent binge eating without regular compensatory behaviors.
11. What electrolyte risk is high-yield in bulimia with vomiting?
Hypokalemia with metabolic alkalosis is high-yield and can create arrhythmia risk.
12. What separates panic disorder from generalized anxiety disorder?
Panic disorder involves recurrent panic attacks and fear or behavior change around future attacks. GAD involves persistent broad worry and tension across domains.
13. What makes PTSD distinct from anxiety disorders?
PTSD is linked to trauma exposure and includes intrusion, avoidance, negative mood/cognition changes, and hyperarousal tied to trauma reminders.
14. What is the OCD cycle?
Intrusive obsessions create distress or threat/error signals. Compulsions temporarily reduce distress, which reinforces the compulsion and keeps the cycle active.
15. Why does DSM-5 separation of PTSD and OCD matter?
It prevents treating all fear or intrusive-thought disorders as the same process. PTSD emphasizes trauma memory and threat conditioning; OCD emphasizes obsessions, compulsions, and cortico-striatal loop dysregulation.
16. Are binge eating and overeating synonymous?
No. Binge eating involves a discrete episode with loss of control and distress; overeating can occur without the same loss-of-control pattern.
17. What is the key difference between bulimia nervosa and binge eating disorder?
Bulimia nervosa includes compensatory behaviors after binge eating, while binge eating disorder does not include regular compensatory behaviors.
18. According to minority stress framing, what raises eating disorder risk in LGBTQ+ individuals?
Stigma, discrimination, bullying, lack of social support, mental health comorbidity, and lack of inclusive health care can increase risk.
19. What behavior is Russell's sign associated with?
Self-induced vomiting causing calluses or injury on the knuckles or back of the hand.
20. What brain change pattern best describes PTSD-related corticolimbic dysfunction?
Reduced hippocampal function, decreased medial prefrontal cortex/anterior cingulate activity, and increased amygdala reactivity.
21. What neurotransmitter changes contribute to PTSD hyperarousal and sleep disturbance?
Increased norepinephrine and orexin activity, plus excitatory-inhibitory imbalance involving glutamate and GABA, can contribute to hyperarousal, hypervigilance, intrusive memories, and sleep disturbance.
22. How is HPA-axis function often described in PTSD?
PTSD may involve dysregulated feedback with often reduced cortisol output despite ongoing stress activation, weakening anti-inflammatory control while sympathetic activation remains high.
23. What are the DSM-5 PTSD symptom clusters?
Intrusion, avoidance, negative alterations in cognition and mood, and marked alterations in arousal and reactivity.

Course Practice Quiz

Instructor-provided question bankThese 15 questions and keyed answers come from Module8_Patho_Practice_Quiz.docx. Open each item to check the course answer after committing to a choice.
24. According to the monoamine hypothesis of depression: A) decreased GABA production; B) increased glutamate production causes mania; C) deficient norepinephrine, dopamine, and serotonin; D) none.
Course key: C. The monoamine hypothesis links depression with reduced signaling involving norepinephrine, dopamine, and serotonin.
25. Which statement about schizophrenia symptoms is false? A) A delusion is a persistent belief contrary to rational judgment or background; B) Disorganized speech is a negative symptom; C) A hallucination occurs without external sensory stimulation; D) Affective flattening is near-absence of emotional or facial expression.
Course key: B. Disorganized speech is categorized as a positive/disorganization symptom, not a negative symptom.
26. Which statement about schizophrenia is correct? A) More common in women; B) Mainly a Western-culture disorder; C) Peak onset age is 45-55; D) Linked to neuroanatomical changes including enlarged ventricles.
Course key: D. Ventricular enlargement is one of the neuroanatomical alterations associated with schizophrenia.
27. HPA-axis activation results in secretion of: A) aldosterone; B) norepinephrine; C) cortisol; D) serotonin.
Course key: C. CRH stimulates ACTH release, which stimulates adrenal cortisol secretion.
28. Anorexia nervosa is characterized by: A) fear of weight gain despite weight loss; B) altered body-image perception; C) amenorrhea in females; D) all are correct.
Course key: D. This reflects the supplied quiz wording. Diagnostic nuance: amenorrhea can occur with energy deficiency but is not required for a current DSM-5/DSM-5-TR diagnosis.
29. Schizophrenia is characterized by: A) excessive persistent worry; B) unremitting sadness and despair; C) disorganized thinking; D) fixation on body image.
Course key: C. Disorganized thinking is a core feature of schizophrenia-spectrum illness.
30. Which statement about bulimia nervosa is incorrect? A) It causes a drastic reduction in body weight; B) Patients often have body-image preoccupation; C) It is more common in females; D) Purging can damage teeth and the esophagus.
Course key: A. People with bulimia nervosa are not necessarily underweight; recurrent purging can cause dental and esophageal injury.
31. Which behavior would you not expect during a manic episode? A) Avolition; B) Poor judgment; C) Delusions; D) Hallucinations.
Course key: A. Mania typically increases drive and activity; avolition is a reduction in motivation associated more strongly with negative symptoms of schizophrenia or severe depression.
32. Which OCD statement is incorrect? A) Compulsions may be behaviors or mental acts; B) Average onset is age 40-50; C) Obsessions may be thoughts, images, ideas, or impulses; D) People often recognize the irrational or excessive nature of symptoms.
Course key: B. OCD usually begins much earlier than age 40-50, commonly in childhood, adolescence, or early adulthood.
33. Generalized anxiety disorder may cause: A) restlessness; B) difficulty concentrating; C) increased muscle tension; D) all are correct.
Course key: D. Restlessness, impaired concentration, and muscle tension are all common manifestations.
34. Which is a positive symptom of schizophrenia? A) Social withdrawal; B) Flat affect; C) Lack of speech; D) Hallucinations.
Course key: D. Hallucinations are positive symptoms; withdrawal, flat affect, and reduced speech are negative symptoms.
35. Intrusive flashbacks, sleep difficulty, detachment, and heightened startle most strongly indicate: A) PTSD; B) panic disorder; C) GAD; D) bipolar disorder.
Course key: A. The combination reflects PTSD intrusion, negative mood/cognition, and hyperarousal clusters.
36. Hallucinations and paranoia in schizophrenia are associated with excessive activation of which receptor? A) Dopamine D2; B) GABA-B; C) alpha-adrenergic; D) nicotinic acetylcholine.
Course key: A. Excessive mesolimbic dopamine D2 signaling is the classic high-yield association with positive symptoms.
37. Tachycardia is a common finding in: A) panic disorder; B) GAD; C) OCD; D) bipolar disorder.
Course key: A. Acute sympathetic activation during a panic attack commonly produces tachycardia.
38. Panic disorder is associated in the supplied course quiz with fewer receptors for which neurotransmitter in the hippocampus, insula, and prefrontal cortex? A) norepinephrine; B) acetylcholine; C) dopamine; D) GABA.
Course key: D. Reduced inhibitory GABA signaling is the course emphasis for panic-disorder circuitry.
