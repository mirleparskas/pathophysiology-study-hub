McCance 9th ed. pp. 202-220: Innate Immunity, Acute Inflammation, Chronic Inflammation

Innate Immunity and Barriers

  • Innate immunity is rapid, nonspecific, and present before exposure; it recognizes broad danger patterns rather than one exact antigen.
  • First-line defenses include intact skin, mucous membranes, normal flora/microbiome, low pH, secretions, enzymes, cilia, coughing, sneezing, and flushing actions.
  • The microbiome helps prevent pathogen colonization, supports immune development, and competes for nutrients and attachment sites.
  • Barrier disruption from wounds, burns, invasive devices, poor perfusion, moisture, or inflammation increases infection risk.
  • Second-line defense is inflammation: it activates when barriers fail or tissue injury occurs.

Recognition and Mediators

  • Innate immune cells recognize PAMPs from microbes and DAMPs from damaged host cells.
  • Pattern-recognition receptors trigger mediator release, phagocytosis, complement activation, and cytokine signalling.
  • Key resident cells: mast cells, macrophages, dendritic cells, tissue lymphocytes, endothelial cells.
  • Histamine causes vasodilation and increased vascular permeability.
  • Prostaglandins and bradykinin contribute to pain; pyrogenic cytokines contribute to fever.
  • Cytokines such as TNF-alpha, IL-1, and IL-6 amplify inflammation and produce systemic effects.
  • Chemokines recruit leukocytes toward the injured or infected site.

Plasma Protein Systems

  • Complement helps destroy pathogens through opsonization, inflammation, chemotaxis, and membrane attack complex formation.
  • Opsonization tags microbes so phagocytes can bind and ingest them more effectively.
  • The clotting system limits spread of infection and starts repair by creating a fibrin framework.
  • The kinin system promotes vasodilation, vascular permeability, and pain through bradykinin.
  • These systems interact; excessive activation can worsen edema, pain, thrombosis, or systemic inflammation.

Cellular Response

  • Leukocytes move from blood to tissue through margination, adhesion, transmigration, and chemotaxis.
  • Neutrophils are the major early responders in acute bacterial inflammation.
  • Neutrophils kill through phagocytosis, enzymes, reactive oxygen species, and NETs.
  • Monocytes arrive later and become macrophages; they clean debris, release cytokines, present antigen, and coordinate repair.
  • Dendritic cells link innate and adaptive immunity by presenting antigen to T cells.
  • Exudate contains fluid, proteins, cells, and debris; pus is rich in dead neutrophils and liquefied tissue debris.

Acute Inflammation

  • Acute inflammation is protective and usually short-lived.
  • Main purposes: remove the trigger, limit tissue damage, prevent spread, and start repair.
  • Classic local signs: redness, heat, swelling, pain, and loss of function.
  • Redness/heat come from vasodilation; swelling comes from increased permeability; pain comes from edema plus pain mediators.
  • Systemic signs can include fever, leukocytosis, malaise, increased CRP, and increased fibrinogen.
  • Possible outcomes: complete resolution, abscess formation, scarring/fibrosis, or progression to chronic inflammation.

Chronic Inflammation

  • Chronic inflammation lasts longer and is less efficient than acute inflammation.
  • Common causes: persistent infection, foreign bodies, chronic irritants, toxic exposures, autoimmune disease, and unresolved acute inflammation.
  • Main cells: macrophages, lymphocytes, plasma cells, and fibroblasts.
  • Consequences: tissue destruction, fibrosis, impaired function, chronic pain, fatigue, and increased malignancy risk in some tissues.
  • Granulomas form when macrophages and lymphocytes wall off material that cannot be eliminated, such as mycobacteria or foreign material.
  • Granulomas can protect by containing the trigger, but they can also distort tissue and impair organ function.

McCance 9th ed. pp. 231-251: Antigens, Humoral Immunity, Cell-Mediated Immunity

Antigens and Antigen Presentation

  • Antigens are molecules that can be recognized by immune receptors and may trigger adaptive immune responses.
  • Each antigen can contain multiple epitopes; epitopes are the specific parts recognized by antibodies or T-cell receptors.
  • Professional APCs include dendritic cells, macrophages, and B cells.
  • MHC molecules present antigen fragments to T cells; this is how T cells "see" antigen.
  • Costimulation and cytokine context matter; antigen recognition without proper signals can lead to tolerance rather than activation.
  • Clonal selection expands lymphocytes that recognize the antigen.
  • Memory cells allow faster and stronger secondary responses.

Humoral Immunity

  • Humoral immunity is B-cell and antibody mediated.
  • Best for extracellular pathogens, toxins, and free viral particles before cell entry.
  • B cells can differentiate into plasma cells that secrete antibodies and memory B cells that persist after the response.
  • Antibodies neutralize toxins/viruses, opsonize microbes, activate complement, and block attachment.
  • IgM appears early in primary responses; IgG is common in blood and tissues and is important for long-term protection.
  • IgA protects mucosal surfaces and appears in secretions; IgE participates in allergy and parasite responses.
  • Vaccines rely heavily on humoral memory but may also stimulate cellular immunity depending on vaccine type.

Cell-Mediated Immunity

  • Cell-mediated immunity is T-cell mediated and is central for intracellular pathogens, infected cells, tumor cells, and transplant rejection.
  • CD4+ T helper cells coordinate immune responses through cytokines and help activate B cells, macrophages, and cytotoxic T cells.
  • CD8+ cytotoxic T cells kill infected or abnormal cells through apoptosis-inducing mechanisms.
  • Regulatory T cells help limit immune responses and maintain self-tolerance.
  • Failure of T-cell function increases risk for viral, fungal, mycobacterial, and opportunistic infections.
  • Adaptive immunity is powerful but can cause disease when misdirected, as in allergy, autoimmunity, alloimmunity, and immune complex injury.

McCance 9th ed. pp. 255-275: Hypersensitivity and Deficiencies in Immunity

Hypersensitivity Overview

  • Hypersensitivity is an immune response that damages host tissue.
  • Sensitization means first exposure creates antigen-specific antibodies or memory T cells.
  • Type I: IgE and mast-cell mediated; immediate allergy; can cause anaphylaxis.
  • Type II: IgG or IgM against cell/tissue antigens; complement, phagocytosis, or receptor dysfunction.
  • Type III: immune complexes deposit in tissues and activate complement/neutrophils.
  • Type IV: T-cell mediated delayed reaction; no antibody required.
  • The same immune tools that protect against infection can cause pathology when directed at harmless antigen, self-antigen, or deposited immune complexes.

Immunodeficiency

  • Immunodeficiency means immune defenses cannot adequately protect against infection.
  • Primary immunodeficiencies are usually genetic or developmental; secondary immunodeficiencies are acquired and more common.
  • Common secondary causes include HIV, malnutrition, diabetes, malignancy, aging, renal disease, corticosteroids, chemotherapy, biologics, and transplant medications.
  • Clinical clue: recurrent, severe, unusual, prolonged, or opportunistic infections.
  • Different immune pathway defects create different infection patterns: antibody problems often cause recurrent bacterial sinopulmonary infections; T-cell problems predispose to viral/fungal/opportunistic infections.
  • Immunodeficiency can also reduce vaccine response and increase cancer risk.

McCance 9th ed. pp. 281-307: Microorganisms, Infectious Disease, Resistance, Vaccines

Microorganisms and Host Relationship

  • Microorganisms include bacteria, viruses, fungi, protozoa, and parasites.
  • Normal flora can be protective, neutral, or opportunistic depending on location and host status.
  • Colonization means organisms are present without tissue invasion or symptomatic damage.
  • Infection means organisms cause tissue injury, immune activation, or clinical disease.
  • Pathogenicity depends on virulence factors, inoculum size, route of entry, tissue tropism, immune status, and host barriers.
  • Virulence mechanisms include adhesion, invasion, toxins, immune evasion, intracellular survival, biofilms, and tissue-destructive enzymes.

Clinical Course of Infection

  • Exposure does not always lead to infection; host defenses may clear the organism.
  • Incubation: organism replicates before symptoms appear.
  • Prodrome: nonspecific symptoms such as malaise, fatigue, low-grade fever, or aches.
  • Illness/invasion: organism-specific signs and inflammatory manifestations are most evident.
  • Convalescence: immune control and tissue repair occur; fatigue may persist.
  • Latency: some organisms persist silently and can reactivate later.
  • Severe infection can progress to bacteremia/viremia, sepsis, shock, organ dysfunction, or death.

Antimicrobials and Resistance

  • Antibiotics work by targeting bacterial cell walls/membranes, protein synthesis, DNA/RNA synthesis, or metabolic pathways.
  • Antibiotics do not treat viral infection unless there is bacterial coinfection.
  • Resistance can be intrinsic or acquired.
  • Acquired resistance can spread through mutation or horizontal gene transfer.
  • Resistance mechanisms include drug degradation, drug modification, efflux pumps, decreased permeability, altered target sites, and bypass pathways.
  • Clinical consequences include treatment failure, longer illness, broader-spectrum therapy, toxicity, higher cost, and increased mortality.
  • Stewardship reduces unnecessary exposure and selection pressure.

Vaccines and Passive Immunization

  • Vaccines expose the immune system to antigen safely enough to create protective memory.
  • Protection can involve antibodies, T-cell responses, or both.
  • Boosters strengthen secondary responses and maintain protective immunity.
  • Live attenuated vaccines can create strong immunity but may be contraindicated in pregnancy or severe immunocompromise.
  • Inactivated/subunit/toxoid/conjugate vaccines cannot replicate but may need boosters or adjuvants.
  • Passive immunization gives preformed antibodies for immediate short-term protection after exposure or when active response is inadequate.
  • Herd immunity reduces transmission when enough people are immune.

McCance 9th ed. pp. 485-487: Pathogenesis of Fever

  • Fever is a regulated rise in body temperature caused by an increased hypothalamic set point.
  • Exogenous pyrogens, such as microbial products, stimulate immune cells.
  • Endogenous pyrogens include cytokines such as IL-1, IL-6, TNF-alpha, and interferons.
  • These mediators increase prostaglandin signalling in the hypothalamus, raising the temperature set point.
  • Chills and shivering occur while the body tries to reach the new set point.
  • Sweating and flushing occur when the set point falls and the body dissipates heat.
  • Moderate fever can support immune function, but high or prolonged fever increases metabolic demand, fluid loss, discomfort, and risk in vulnerable patients.
  • Assess fever in context: age, immune status, source symptoms, hemodynamics, mental status, hydration, and sepsis risk matter more than the number alone.

1. Introduction to Epidemiology

Incidence vs Prevalence

TermMeaningClinical use
IncidenceNew cases in a defined population over a defined time.Best for risk, causation, outbreaks, prevention planning.
PrevalenceAll existing cases in a defined population at a point or period of time.Best for disease burden, staffing, screening demand, chronic care planning.
Point prevalenceExisting cases at one moment.Example: number of inpatients with C. difficile today.
Period prevalenceExisting cases during a time window.Example: number of people treated for asthma this year.
Exam trapIncidence counts new cases only. Prevalence rises when disease lasts longer, even if new cases are not increasing.

Treatment Logic

  • Antibiotics target bacterial structures or pathways: cell wall/membrane function, protein synthesis, DNA replication, or folate metabolism.
  • Vaccines prime adaptive immunity before exposure so memory B and T cells respond faster and more strongly.
  • Passive immunization gives preformed antibodies for immediate short-term protection, useful when there is no time to build active immunity.
  • Supportive care protects perfusion, oxygenation, hydration, nutrition, pain control, and organ function while the immune system and targeted therapy work.

4. Chronic Inflammation

Causes and Consequences

  • Persistent organisms that evade host defense, such as mycobacteria.
  • Ongoing exposure to toxins, chemicals, particulate matter, or physical irritants.
  • Autoimmune or dysregulated immune responses.
  • Unresolved acute inflammation.
  • Idiopathic inflammation when the cause is unknown.

Consequences include tissue destruction, fibrosis, altered organ function, chronic pain, fatigue, impaired healing, and increased cancer risk through cytokines, ROS, DNA damage, and proliferative repair signals.

Granuloma Development

Persistent triggerAgent resists clearance.
MacrophagesAccumulate and may fuse into giant cells.
LymphocytesForm a surrounding immune wall.
FibrosisCollagen capsule may isolate the area.

A granuloma walls off material the immune system cannot easily remove. It limits spread but can also damage tissue and impair organ function.

FeatureAcute inflammationChronic inflammation
TimingSeconds to minutes onset; usually days.Two weeks or longer.
Main cellsNeutrophils, then macrophages.Macrophages, lymphocytes, plasma cells.
PurposeNeutralize threat and start repair.Response to persistent threat or dysregulation.
Tissue outcomeResolution, regeneration, abscess, or scar.Tissue destruction, remodeling, fibrosis, granuloma.

7. Immunodeficiency

Primary vs Secondary

TypeCauseClinical clue
PrimaryUsually genetic or developmental immune defect.Unusual, recurrent, severe infections from early life.
SecondaryIllness, medication, malnutrition, aging, HIV, cancer therapy, diabetes, renal disease.More common; risk depends on underlying cause and immune pathway affected.

Consequences include opportunistic infection, poor vaccine response, prolonged infection, unusual organisms, reactivation of latent infection, malignancy risk, and poor wound healing.

HIV Mechanism

  1. HIV gp120 binds CD4 on T helper cells and uses coreceptors for entry.
  2. Viral RNA enters the cell.
  3. Reverse transcriptase converts RNA into DNA.
  4. Integrase inserts viral DNA into the host genome.
  5. Virus can remain latent or replicate, destroying CD4 T helper cells directly and indirectly.

Clinical Course of HIV

Acute infectionHigh viral load, CD4 decline, nonspecific flu-like illness.
→
Immune responseViral load drops; CD4 count partly recovers.
→
Clinical latencyVirus persists, often years, with gradual immune damage.
→
AIDSSevere CD4 depletion, often below 200 cells/uL, with opportunistic infections or cancers.
Clinical assessmentThink infection history, oral thrush, weight loss, lymphadenopathy, fever/night sweats, respiratory symptoms, neurologic changes, skin lesions, medication adherence, viral load, CD4 count, STI screening, and psychosocial barriers to treatment.

8. Sickness Behaviour: Fever and Cachexia

Fever

During acute infection, exogenous pyrogens such as bacterial LPS and endogenous cytokines such as TNF-alpha and IL-1 act on the hypothalamus. The thermoregulatory set point rises, producing chills and heat-conserving behaviours until the body reaches the new set point.

AssessTemperature trend, heart rate, blood pressure, respiratory rate, oxygenation, rigors, mental status, hydration, source symptoms, immune status, cultures/labs as ordered, and response to antipyretics or antimicrobials.

Cachexia

Cachexia is a complex metabolic syndrome with muscle wasting, weakness, fatigue, anorexia, and sometimes fat loss. It is driven by inflammation, altered metabolism, reduced intake, inefficient energy production, increased protein breakdown, impaired regeneration, and hormonal appetite changes.

Key distinctionCachexia is not simple starvation. Nutrition matters, but inflammation and metabolic reprogramming make weight and muscle loss difficult to reverse with calories alone.
Cachexia contributorMechanismClinical relevance
TNF-alpha, IL-1, other cytokinesPromote catabolism and appetite changes.Weight loss, fatigue, weakness, poor treatment tolerance.
Skeletal muscle proteolysisIncreased myofibril protein degradation and apoptosis.Reduced mobility, falls risk, respiratory weakness.
Fat metabolism changesIncreased lipolysis and impaired lipid synthesis.Loss of fat stores and energy reserve.
Tumor/treatment factorsTumor metabolism, chemotherapy effects, nausea, taste changes, inflammation.Need symptom control, nutrition support, function-focused care.

Systemic Lupus Erythematosus

SLE is a chronic autoimmune inflammatory disease with loss of immune tolerance, autoantibody production, immune complex deposition, complement activation, and multi-organ inflammation. It can affect skin, joints, kidneys, nervous system, and hematologic systems.

Risk + triggerGenetic susceptibility, estrogen, UV radiation, infection, smoking, chemicals, stress, sleep deprivation.
→
Innate activationPoor apoptotic debris clearance exposes nuclear antigens; TLRs and plasmacytoid dendritic cells drive interferon-alpha.
→
Adaptive loss of toleranceBAFF supports autoreactive B cells; T-cell regulation is impaired.
→
Immune complexesAutoantibodies bind nuclear antigens, deposit in tissue, activate complement.
→
Clinical diseaseFlares/remissions, rash, arthritis, nephritis, neuropsychiatric disease, cytopenias, organ damage.
Clinical focusWhat to connect
AssessmentFlares, photosensitivity, rash, joint pain/swelling, fatigue, renal symptoms, neuro symptoms, CBC, urinalysis, creatinine, complement, autoantibodies.
Treatment logicHydroxychloroquine reduces flares; corticosteroids control acute inflammation but add toxicity; immunosuppressants reduce broad immune activation.
Targeted therapiesBelimumab reduces BAFF-driven autoreactive B cells; anifrolumab blocks type I interferon signaling; voclosporin reduces T-cell activation in lupus nephritis.

Measles

Measles is a highly infectious airborne systemic viral illness caused by a Paramyxovirus. It begins in the respiratory tract, spreads through lymphoid tissue and blood, then causes respiratory, conjunctival, mucosal, and skin manifestations.

ExposureAirborne droplets/aerosols or close contact; contagious before and after rash onset.
→
EntryVirus infects respiratory dendritic cells, alveolar macrophages, and lymphocytes through CD150 receptors.
→
SpreadReplication in lung and lymphoid tissues causes viremia and systemic infection.
→
Immune injuryCytokines, chemokines, complement, and T-cell infiltration produce fever, cough, coryza, conjunctivitis, Koplik spots, and rash.
→
PreventionMMR vaccination creates protective antibodies, T cells, and memory without causing disease.
Clinical pointWhat to know
Classic manifestationsIncubation about 7-21 days; prodrome 2-4 days; fever, cough, coryza, conjunctivitis, Koplik spots, then descending rash.
ComplicationsOtitis media, pneumonia, diarrhea, encephalitis, vision loss, death; risk is higher in young children, unvaccinated people, pregnancy, and immunocompromise.
Diagnosis/treatmentNP/throat swab, measles IgM, urine testing as directed by public health; treatment is supportive, with vitamin A for hospitalized children per guidance.
PreventionAirborne precautions, N95, eye protection, gown/gloves, isolation, vaccination, and careful vaccine-hesitancy communication.

Clinical Practice Quick Cards

When You See Infection

  • Ask onset, exposure, travel, sick contacts, wounds, devices, immunosuppression.
  • Assess vitals, source signs, pain, mental status, hydration, perfusion.
  • Watch for sepsis: fever or hypothermia, tachycardia, tachypnea, hypotension, confusion, low urine output.

When You See Inflammation

  • Local: redness, heat, swelling, pain, loss of function, drainage.
  • Systemic: fever, fatigue, leukocytosis, elevated CRP/fibrinogen.
  • Differentiate protective acute response from persistent chronic damage.

When You See Immune Dysfunction

  • Recurrent, severe, unusual, or opportunistic infections suggest immunodeficiency.
  • Timing after exposure helps classify hypersensitivity.
  • Medication history matters: steroids, chemotherapy, biologics, transplant drugs.

Practice and Active Recall

1. A disease has many long-lasting cases but few new cases per year. Which measure is high: incidence or prevalence?
Prevalence. Chronic duration increases the number of existing cases even if incidence is low.
2. Why does acute inflammation cause heat and redness?
Vasodilation increases blood flow to the affected tissue.
3. Which cells arrive first during acute inflammation?
Neutrophils are the first major recruited leukocytes, followed by monocytes/macrophages.
4. Why can chronic inflammation promote cancer?
Persistent cytokines, ROS, DNA damage, tissue repair signalling, and chronic infection can increase mutation risk and abnormal proliferation.
5. What does sensitization mean in hypersensitivity?
Initial exposure creates antigen-specific antibodies and/or memory T cells, so later exposure produces an exaggerated damaging response.
6. Compare Type I and Type IV hypersensitivity.
Type I is immediate, IgE/mast-cell mediated, often allergic. Type IV is delayed, T-cell mediated, usually develops over 48-72 hours.
7. How does HIV target CD4 T helper cells?
The viral gp120 protein binds CD4, HIV enters the cell, reverse transcriptase converts RNA to DNA, and integrase inserts viral DNA into the host genome.
8. Why are vaccines different from passive immunization?
Vaccines stimulate the patient to make active adaptive immunity and memory. Passive immunization gives preformed antibodies for immediate but temporary protection.
9. Name three bacterial resistance mechanisms.
Examples: drug-degrading enzymes, drug-modifying enzymes, efflux pumps, altered permeability, altered target sites, horizontal gene transfer.
10. Why is cachexia not the same as simple starvation?
Cachexia includes inflammation-driven metabolic changes, muscle proteolysis, inefficient energy production, impaired regeneration, and appetite/hormonal changes. Calories alone may not reverse it.
