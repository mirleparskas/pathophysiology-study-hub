McCance 9th ed. pp. 1504-1512: Structure and Function of the Skin

Skin Layers and Barrier Function

  • The skin is the largest organ and protects against trauma, pathogens, chemicals, UV radiation, and fluid loss.
  • The epidermis is avascular and depends on diffusion from the dermis.
  • The stratum corneum is the outer barrier; keratinized cells and lipids limit water loss and microbial entry.
  • Keratinocytes are the dominant epidermal cells and produce keratin for structural protection.
  • The dermis contains collagen, elastin, blood vessels, lymphatics, nerves, hair follicles, glands, and immune cells.
  • The subcutaneous layer provides insulation, cushioning, energy storage, and a route for vessels/nerves.
  • Barrier breakdown from dryness, maceration, trauma, inflammation, or poor perfusion increases infection risk.

Skin Cells and Functions

  • Melanocytes produce melanin, which absorbs UV radiation and contributes to pigmentation.
  • Langerhans cells are dendritic antigen-presenting cells; they connect skin exposure to adaptive immunity.
  • Merkel cells contribute to light-touch sensation.
  • Fibroblasts produce dermal extracellular matrix, including collagen and elastin.
  • Sebaceous glands produce sebum; excess sebum and follicular plugging contribute to acne.
  • Sweat glands support thermoregulation and excretion.
  • Skin participates in vitamin D synthesis after UV exposure.

Clinical Assessment Foundation

  • Describe lesions by type, size, colour, shape, arrangement, distribution, border, elevation, fluid, scale, crust, ulceration, warmth, pain, and itch.
  • Primary lesions are the first visible change; secondary lesions develop from scratching, infection, healing, chronicity, or treatment.
  • Distribution matters: dermatomal, exposed-area, flexural, extensor, intertriginous, follicular, mucosal, acral, and generalized patterns suggest different causes.
  • Skin colour changes may be subtle in darker skin; assess warmth, edema, texture, tenderness, and patient-reported changes.

McCance 9th ed. pp. 1513-1516: Inflammatory Disorders of the Skin

Dermatitis/Eczema Pattern

  • Dermatitis involves inflammation of skin with pruritus, erythema, vesicles, scaling, crusting, or lichenification depending on stage.
  • Pruritus drives scratching, which worsens barrier injury and can cause excoriation, infection, and thickened skin.
  • Inflammation can be acute with edema/vesicles or chronic with scale and lichenification.
  • Barrier dysfunction increases allergen entry and microbial colonization.

Atopic vs Contact Dermatitis

  • Atopic dermatitis is chronic/relapsing and linked with barrier impairment, allergy tendency, asthma, family history, and IgE elevation.
  • Atopic dermatitis often presents with intense itch, dry skin, erythema, scaling, excoriations, and lichenification.
  • Allergic contact dermatitis is a type IV delayed T-cell reaction to a contact antigen/hapten.
  • Contact dermatitis usually follows the exposure pattern and may produce erythema, edema, vesicles, oozing, crusting, and itch.
  • Irritant contact dermatitis is direct barrier injury and does not require immune sensitization.
  • Clinical workup depends on timeline, distribution, exposures, occupation, products, metals, plants, topical medications, and recurrence.

McCance 9th ed. pp. 1516-1519: Papulosquamous Disorders

Psoriasis

  • Psoriasis is immune-mediated and involves excessive keratinocyte proliferation with abnormal differentiation.
  • Th1/Th17 pathways, dendritic cells, cytokines, and genetic susceptibility contribute.
  • Lesions are usually well-demarcated erythematous plaques with silvery scale.
  • Common sites include scalp, elbows, knees, trunk, and other extensor surfaces.
  • Nails and joints can be involved; ask about nail pitting, joint pain, stiffness, and swelling.
  • Triggers can include trauma, infection, stress, medications, alcohol, smoking, and systemic inflammation.

Rosacea, Acne, Lichen Planus

  • Acne rosacea is a chronic inflammatory facial disorder with flushing, central facial erythema, telangiectasia, papules/pustules, and possible ocular involvement.
  • Rosacea is not comedonal; blackheads/whiteheads point more toward acne vulgaris.
  • Acne vulgaris involves follicular hyperkeratinization, sebum, Cutibacterium acnes, androgen effects, and inflammation.
  • Closed comedones can rupture and trigger inflammatory papules, pustules, nodules, or cysts.
  • Lichen planus is immune-mediated and often presents with pruritic, purple, polygonal, flat-topped papules; mucosal involvement can occur.
  • For all papulosquamous disorders, note distribution, scale, itch/pain, triggers, mucosal/nail/joint involvement, and psychosocial impact.

McCance 9th ed. pp. 1521-1522, 1544: Bacterial Infections and Impetigo

Folliculitis and Cellulitis

  • Skin normally resists infection through barrier integrity, microbiome competition, immune surveillance, pH, dryness, and shedding.
  • Folliculitis is infection/inflammation of hair follicles, commonly caused by S. aureus.
  • Folliculitis appears as follicle-centered papules or pustules with erythema; risk rises with shaving, occlusion, moisture, friction, and trauma.
  • Cellulitis is acute infection of dermis/subcutaneous tissue, commonly caused by streptococci and S. aureus.
  • Cellulitis presents with warmth, erythema, edema, pain, tenderness, and spreading poorly demarcated borders; fever or lymphangitis may occur.
  • Risk factors include skin breaks, tinea pedis, ulcers, edema, venous disease, lymphedema, diabetes, immunosuppression, and prior cellulitis.
  • Rapid progression, severe pain, bullae, necrosis, hypotension, or altered mental status are urgent warning signs.

Impetigo

  • Impetigo is a superficial, contagious bacterial infection common in children and close-contact settings.
  • Common organisms: S. aureus, S. pyogenes, or both.
  • Non-bullous impetigo often begins as vesicles/pustules that rupture and form honey-coloured crusts.
  • Bullous impetigo is usually toxin-producing S. aureus; toxins disrupt epidermal adhesion and create flaccid bullae.
  • Ecthyma is a deeper ulcerative form that extends into the dermis and can scar.
  • Transmission prevention: hand hygiene, cover lesions, avoid sharing towels/clothing, clean surfaces, treat close contacts/outbreaks as directed.

McCance 9th ed. pp. 1523-1524, 1546-1549: Viral Infections

Varicella-Zoster and Herpes Simplex

  • Varicella-zoster virus causes chickenpox as primary infection and shingles after reactivation from latency.
  • Chickenpox presents with pruritic vesicles in different stages, often beginning on trunk/face/scalp.
  • Shingles follows a unilateral dermatomal pattern with pain/tingling before grouped vesicles appear.
  • Postherpetic neuralgia can follow shingles, especially in older adults.
  • Herpes simplex virus causes recurrent grouped painful vesicles, commonly oral or genital.
  • HSV establishes latency in sensory ganglia and reactivates with stress, illness, UV exposure, trauma, or immune suppression.

Human Papilloma Virus

  • HPV infects epithelial cells and can cause warts, papillomas, dysplasia, or cancer depending on strain and site.
  • Low-risk types commonly cause benign warts; high-risk types are associated with cervical, anogenital, and oropharyngeal cancers.
  • Transmission occurs through skin-to-skin or sexual contact depending on type/site.
  • Persistence of high-risk HPV is more concerning than transient infection.
  • Vaccination prevents infection with major cancer-associated and wart-associated HPV types.
  • Screening and follow-up remain important because vaccination does not treat existing infection.

McCance 9th ed. pp. 1524-1525, 1545-1546: Fungal Infections

Tinea / Dermatophytes

  • Dermatophytes digest keratin and infect skin, hair, and nails.
  • Tinea infections are named by location: capitis, corporis, cruris, pedis, and unguium.
  • Warmth, moisture, occlusion, shared surfaces, close contact, and minor skin trauma increase risk.
  • Tinea corporis often forms annular scaly plaques with central clearing.
  • Tinea pedis may cause interdigital scaling, maceration, fissures, itch, and secondary bacterial entry.
  • Onychomycosis causes thickened, discoloured, dystrophic nails and can mimic psoriatic nail disease.
  • Diagnosis can be clinical but scraping/culture helps confirm uncertain or persistent cases.

Candidiasis

  • Candida albicans is a yeast that can be normal flora but becomes pathogenic when host defenses or local conditions change.
  • Risk factors include antibiotics, diabetes, pregnancy, immunosuppression, steroids, moisture, occlusion, and mucosal disruption.
  • Cutaneous candidiasis often affects skin folds with moist erythema, maceration, soreness, and satellite lesions.
  • Oral candidiasis produces white plaques that may scrape off and leave erythematous mucosa.
  • Vaginal candidiasis causes pruritus, irritation, erythema, and thick discharge.
  • Invasive candidiasis is a serious systemic infection risk in severely immunocompromised or critically ill patients.

McCance 9th ed. pp. 371-373, 1529-1533: Cancer Diagnosis/Staging and Skin Cancer

Cancer Concepts

  • Cancer diagnosis depends on identifying abnormal growth, invasion, cellular atypia, and tissue origin.
  • Grading describes how abnormal/aggressive the tumor cells appear.
  • Staging describes extent of disease and is more directly tied to prognosis and treatment planning.
  • TNM logic: tumor size/local invasion, nodal involvement, and metastasis.
  • Benign tumors are localized and noninvasive; malignant tumors invade and can metastasize.
  • Early detection matters because local disease is usually easier to treat than metastatic disease.

UV Damage and Benign/Premalignant Lesions

  • UV radiation damages DNA and contributes to mutations in oncogenes, tumor suppressor genes, and DNA repair pathways.
  • UVB directly damages DNA; UVA contributes to oxidative injury and deeper skin damage.
  • Actinic keratosis is a premalignant UV-related keratinocyte lesion that can progress to squamous cell carcinoma.
  • Nevi are benign melanocyte proliferations, but changing nevi require melanoma assessment.
  • Use ABCDE for pigmented lesions: asymmetry, border irregularity, colour variation, diameter, evolving.
  • Assess skin cancer risk in all skin tones; darker skin reduces UV-related risk but does not eliminate risk and can delay recognition.

BCC vs SCC vs Melanoma

  • Basal cell carcinoma arises from basal/stem cells, is the most common, grows slowly, rarely metastasizes, but can invade locally.
  • BCC often appears pearly/translucent with rolled borders, telangiectasia, central depression, crusting, or ulceration.
  • Squamous cell carcinoma arises from keratinocytes and has greater metastatic potential than BCC.
  • SCC may appear as a firm, scaly, crusted, ulcerated, or bleeding lesion, often on sun-exposed skin or chronic wounds.
  • Melanoma arises from melanocytes and has the highest metastatic risk; it can arise from a nevus or de novo.
  • Melanoma may be asymmetric, irregularly bordered, variably coloured, enlarging, bleeding, itchy, painful, or simply evolving.
  • In people of colour, acral/nail/mucosal sites and chronically injured or scarred skin deserve careful inspection.

Measles Presentation Pearls

  • Measles is also called rubeola and is caused by measles virus, not rubella.
  • Transmission is highly contagious through respiratory droplets and airborne spread; infection-control and public health reporting matter.
  • Classic pattern: prodrome followed by Koplik spots and a descending maculopapular rash.
  • Complications emphasized: pneumonia is common; encephalitis, blindness, and severe dehydration are less common but serious.
  • High-risk groups include young children, pregnant patients, immunocompromised people, unvaccinated individuals, health-care workers, and people in low-vaccination or outbreak settings.
  • Treatment is mainly supportive; vitamin A matters because measles can deplete vitamin A and worsen ocular/immune complications.
  • Vaccines do not cause measles and do not transmit measles infection; vaccine conversations should use empathy, open-ended questions, and trusted information.

SLE Presentation Pearls

  • SLE was framed as a heterogeneous autoimmune disease with genetic, hormonal, environmental, infectious, and immune-complex drivers.
  • Common manifestations include fatigue, photosensitivity, rash/skin findings, joint symptoms, systemic flares, and possible renal involvement.
  • UV exposure, viral infections such as Epstein-Barr virus, stress, sleep disruption, and environmental exposures can contribute to flares or risk.
  • Autoantibodies and complement levels can help monitor disease activity; impaired immune-complex clearance contributes to tissue injury.
  • Traditional treatments include hydroxychloroquine and corticosteroids; newer agents discussed included belimumab, anifrolumab, and voclosporin.
  • Anifrolumab was linked with increased herpes zoster risk, so patient selection and infection risk matter.
  • Organ-specific therapy aims to improve outcomes while limiting broad immunosuppression toxicity.

2. Inflammatory Disorders of the Skin

Atopic Dermatitis

Atopic dermatitis is a chronic, relapsing inflammatory skin disorder linked with barrier dysfunction, type I allergic tendency, IgE elevation, family history, asthma, and allergies. The slide shorthand is AAA: atopic dermatitis, allergies, asthma.

  • Mechanism: impaired barrier plus immune activation promotes pruritus and inflammation.
  • Manifestations: erythema, pruritus, scaling, dry skin, excoriations, and lichenification with chronic scratching.
  • Pattern: common in infancy/childhood; flexural involvement is common in older children/adults.

Allergic Contact Dermatitis

Allergic contact dermatitis is a type IV delayed, T-cell-mediated hypersensitivity reaction. A small chemical or hapten binds host proteins, Langerhans cells present antigen to T cells, and re-exposure triggers inflammation.

  • Mechanism: delayed cell-mediated reaction, usually 48-72 hours after exposure.
  • Manifestations: localized erythema, swelling, pruritus, vesicles, oozing or crusting in the contact pattern.
  • Common triggers: poison ivy, nickel, fragrances, latex, cosmetics, detergents, fabric softener, occupational chemicals.

Dermatophytes are fungi that thrive on keratin in skin, hair, and nails. Tinea infections spread through direct contact or fomites, especially warm moist environments.

Candidiasis

Candida albicans is normally present on skin, GI tract, and reproductive mucosa, but can become pathogenic with immunosuppression, antibiotics, moisture, occlusion, diabetes, or barrier breakdown.

  • Skin folds: moist, macerated erythematous rash, often with satellite lesions.
  • Oral thrush: whitish-yellow curd-like plaques.
  • Vaginal candidiasis: pruritus, irritation, thick discharge.
  • Severe immunocompromise can allow invasive candidiasis involving blood or organs.
CompareTinea often has scale and ring-like borders. Candida in folds tends to look moist/macerated. Inverse psoriasis is usually drier and well-demarcated.

7. Benign Tumours and Skin Cancer

UV mechanismUVB directly damages DNA, while UVA contributes through oxidative stress and DNA injury. Repeated damage plus impaired repair can activate oncogenes, inactivate tumor suppressors such as p53, and allow malignant transformation.

Skin Cancer in People of Colour

Skin cancer is less common in people with darker skin, but delayed recognition contributes to later-stage diagnosis and worse outcomes. Lesions may occur in less sun-exposed sites, including palms, soles, nail units, mucosa, and chronically injured or inflamed skin.

Clinical Practice Quick Cards

Skin Assessment

  • Inspect full distribution, not just one lesion.
  • Describe lesion type, colour, size, border, elevation, fluid, scale, crust, ulceration, warmth, pain, and itch.
  • Ask onset, exposure, travel, contacts, new products, work hazards, medications, allergies, immune status.

Infection Red Flags

  • Fever, tachycardia, hypotension, confusion, rapidly spreading erythema, severe pain, necrosis, bullae.
  • Diabetes, neuropathy, immunosuppression, lymphedema, venous disease, wounds, bites, devices.
  • Poor response after 24-48 hours may mean resistant organism, abscess, wrong diagnosis, or deeper infection.

Cancer Screening

  • Use ABCDE and also ask whether the lesion is new, changing, bleeding, painful, itchy, or nonhealing.
  • Check sun-exposed and non-sun-exposed sites, including palms, soles, nails, mucosa, and scars.
  • Document size and appearance; refer suspicious lesions promptly.

Practice and Active Recall

1. What is the difference between a macule and a papule?
A macule is flat; a papule is elevated and palpable.
2. What cell presents antigens to T lymphocytes in allergic contact dermatitis?
Langerhans cells, which are dendritic antigen-presenting cells in the epidermis.
3. What type of hypersensitivity is allergic contact dermatitis?
Type IV delayed, cell-mediated hypersensitivity.
4. Which immunoglobulin is commonly elevated in atopic dermatitis?
IgE.
5. What causes bullous impetigo?
A toxin-producing strain of Staphylococcus aureus that disrupts cellular adhesion.
6. What type of infection is tinea corporis?
A dermatophyte fungal infection of the body/trunk, commonly called ringworm.
7. What skin disorder is linked to colonization by Cutibacterium acnes?
Acne vulgaris.
8. What causes chickenpox?
Primary infection with varicella-zoster virus.
9. Which skin cancer has the highest metastatic risk?
Melanoma.
10. Why can skin cancer be diagnosed later in people of colour?
Lower perceived risk, atypical or less sun-exposed locations, subtler visual cues, and lower screening suspicion can delay recognition.
11. What does the atopic triad mean in the dermatitis lecture?
Atopic dermatitis often clusters with asthma, allergic rhinitis, and food allergy.
12. Why can allergic contact dermatitis be delayed after re-exposure?
It is a type IV T-cell mediated response; memory T cells are reactivated and the visible inflammatory response may take 1 to 2 days.
13. What psoriasis types did the lecture emphasize?
Plaque, inverse, guttate, pustular, and psoriatic arthritis-associated disease patterns.
14. Why does impetigo prevention focus heavily on hygiene and contact control?
Impetigo is highly contagious and spreads through close contact, contaminated hands, shared items, and disrupted skin barriers.
15. What measles name should not be confused with rubella?
Rubeola is measles. Rubella is a different virus.
16. What are the classic measles skin/mucosal clues?
Koplik spots followed by a descending maculopapular rash after the prodrome.
17. Why did the SLE presentation emphasize complement and autoantibody monitoring?
They can help monitor immune activity and flares because immune-complex formation and impaired clearance contribute to tissue injury.
18. Which newer SLE therapy was linked with increased herpes zoster risk?
Anifrolumab.
