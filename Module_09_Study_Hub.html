Neurologic Foundations from Lecture

Neuron

Electrically excitable signaling cell. Dendrites receive input, the soma integrates it, and the axon transmits the action potential to synapses.

Neuroglia

Astrocytes support neurons and the blood-brain barrier; oligodendrocytes form CNS myelin; microglia provide immune surveillance; ependymal cells line ventricles.

Myelin

Insulates axons and enables saltatory conduction between nodes of Ranvier. Demyelination slows or blocks conduction and produces distributed neurologic deficits.

Instructor emphasisThe module depends on understanding neurons versus neuroglia, excitatory versus inhibitory input, myelin-dependent conduction, the blood-brain barrier, CSF circulation, and how location determines neurologic manifestations.

4. Demyelinating Diseases and Movement Disorders

DisorderCore mechanismClinical manifestations

Clinical Consult Add-Ins

Myasthenia Gravis: Mechanism to Treatment

  • Type II hypersensitivity: IgG autoantibodies target postsynaptic nicotinic ACh receptors or related proteins such as MuSK/LRP4, reduce the safety margin of neuromuscular transmission, and can activate complement-mediated membrane injury.
  • Pattern: fatigable ocular, bulbar, axial, limb, or respiratory weakness without sensory loss. Ptosis and diplopia are common; heat and repeated activity can worsen weakness.
  • Triggers: infection, surgery, and certain medications may precipitate deterioration. Coexisting autoimmune thyroid disease, rheumatoid arthritis, or lupus may occur.
  • Diagnosis: AChR/MuSK/LRP4 antibodies, repetitive nerve stimulation or single-fiber EMG, bedside ice test for ptosis, and chest imaging to evaluate the thymus.
  • Treatment logic: pyridostigmine increases available ACh; corticosteroids and steroid-sparing agents suppress autoimmunity; IVIG or plasma exchange acts rapidly in major worsening; thymectomy is required for thymoma and may benefit selected generalized AChR-positive disease.
  • Targeted therapy: rituximab depletes B cells; FcRn inhibitors accelerate IgG removal; complement inhibitors protect the postsynaptic membrane in selected antibody-positive generalized MG.

Myasthenic Crisis

  • Respiratory or severe bulbar weakness is an emergency because ventilation and airway protection can fail.
  • Monitor respiratory status rather than waiting for obvious distress; infection is a common precipitant.
  • Historical teaching contrasted myasthenic crisis with cholinergic excess using edrophonium. Modern diagnosis relies more on the clinical pattern, respiratory measures, antibody testing, and electrophysiology because edrophonium testing is not routine and can cause bradycardia or bronchospasm.
  • Energy-intensive activities are often best scheduled after rest and when medication effect is strongest, commonly earlier in the day.

Parkinson Disease

  • Parkinsonism is a syndrome; idiopathic Parkinson disease accounts for most cases.
  • Degeneration of melanin-containing substantia nigra pars compacta neurons depletes dopamine in the nigrostriatal pathway and disrupts direct/indirect basal-ganglia motor circuits.
  • Alpha-synuclein aggregation and Lewy pathology, mitochondrial dysfunction, impaired protein clearance, genetics, and neuroinflammation contribute.
  • Motor pattern: bradykinesia with resting tremor and/or rigidity, initially asymmetric; later postural instability and gait dysfunction. Nonmotor autonomic, sleep, mood, cognitive, and sensory symptoms may precede or accompany motor disease.
  • Levodopa/carbidopa restores dopamine precursor availability; dopamine agonists, MAO-B inhibitors, COMT inhibitors, amantadine, and selected anticholinergics modify symptoms. Deep-brain stimulation can help medication fluctuations or refractory tremor in selected patients.

5. Alteration in Cognition: Delirium and Dementia

FeatureDeliriumAlzheimer disease
Onset/courseAcute to subacute; fluctuates over hours and across the day.Insidious, progressive decline over years.
Core deficitAttention and awareness are impaired.Early episodic memory impairment, followed by broader cognitive and functional decline.
Common causesInfection, medication/toxin, withdrawal, metabolic or organ failure, pain, dehydration, sleep disruption, surgery.Multifactorial neurodegeneration involving age, genetics and vascular/metabolic risk.

Exam trapAn older adult with sudden confusion does not automatically have “worsening dementia.” Acute onset, inattention, and fluctuation point to delirium and demand a search for a precipitating cause.

One-Page Cram Sheet

Mechanisms

  • TBI: primary mechanical injury → secondary excitotoxic/inflammatory injury.
  • Ischemic stroke: occlusion → ATP failure → excitotoxicity → infarct core and penumbra.
  • Hemorrhage: vessel rupture → tissue injury + mass effect + ICP.
  • Meningitis: meningeal/CSF inflammation; encephalitis: brain parenchymal dysfunction.
  • MS, ALS, PD, MG localize to CNS myelin, motor neuron, basal ganglia, and NMJ.

Fast Contrasts

  • TIA has no established infarction; stroke does.
  • Delirium is acute, fluctuating, inattentive; Alzheimer disease is insidious and progressive.
  • Migraine = pulsating + nausea/light sensitivity; cluster = orbital autonomic pain; tension = bilateral pressure.
  • Epidural = arterial/lucid interval; subdural = bridging veins/slower evolution.
  • Normal imaging does not rule out concussion.

Practice and Active Recall

1. What separates focal from diffuse TBI?
Focal injury is localized—such as contusion or hematoma—while diffuse injury involves widespread axonal strain from acceleration/deceleration or rotation.
2. Why can TBI worsen after the initial impact?
Secondary injury adds excitotoxicity, calcium influx, mitochondrial failure, inflammation, edema, ischemia, and raised ICP after the primary mechanical damage.
3. What GCS ranges are commonly used for mild, moderate, and severe TBI?
Mild 13-15, moderate 9-12, and severe 3-8, interpreted with loss of consciousness, amnesia, imaging, and neurologic findings.
4. Why does normal imaging not rule out concussion?
Concussion is primarily a functional and metabolic brain disturbance; routine structural imaging can be normal.
5. How does a TIA differ from cerebral infarction?
A TIA causes transient focal neurologic dysfunction without established acute infarction; cerebral infarction represents irreversible tissue death.
6. Compare thrombotic, embolic, and lacunar ischemic strokes.
Thrombotic strokes form locally on diseased arteries; embolic strokes arise elsewhere and lodge abruptly; lacunar strokes affect small penetrating vessels in deep structures.
7. What is the ischemic penumbra?
Hypoperfused tissue surrounding the infarct core that is dysfunctional but potentially salvageable if blood flow is restored promptly.
8. Which presentation is classic for subarachnoid hemorrhage?
Sudden thunderclap or “worst headache of life,” possibly with vomiting, meningismus, reduced consciousness, or focal findings.
9. What is the fastest pathophysiologic distinction between meningitis and encephalitis?
Meningitis primarily inflames meninges and CSF spaces; encephalitis primarily affects brain parenchyma and therefore prominently alters cognition, behavior, consciousness, or causes seizures.
10. Which organisms are high-yield in bacterial meningitis?
S. pneumoniae and N. meningitidis are major causes beyond the neonatal period; group B streptococcus and E. coli matter in neonates, and Listeria in neonates, older adults, pregnancy, or immunocompromise.
11. Localize MS, ALS, Parkinson disease, and myasthenia gravis.
MS: CNS myelin; ALS: upper and lower motor neurons; Parkinson disease: substantia nigra/basal-ganglia dopamine circuits; MG: postsynaptic neuromuscular junction.
12. What manifestation pattern suggests myasthenia gravis?
Fluctuating fatigable weakness—often ptosis, diplopia, bulbar or proximal weakness—that worsens with use and improves with rest, without sensory loss.
13. What separates delirium from Alzheimer disease at the bedside?
Delirium is acute, fluctuating, and marked by inattention; Alzheimer disease is insidious and progressively impairs memory and other cognitive functions.
14. Compare the three primary headache patterns.
Migraine is often unilateral/pulsatile with nausea and sensory sensitivity; cluster causes severe unilateral orbital pain with autonomic signs; tension-type causes bilateral pressing/tightening pain.
15. What is the epidural versus subdural hematoma shortcut?
Epidural is classically an arterial middle-meningeal bleed with possible lucid interval; subdural is usually venous bridging-vessel bleeding and may evolve more slowly.
16. Why does myasthenia gravis produce fatigable weakness without sensory loss?
Autoantibodies impair postsynaptic neuromuscular transmission, lowering the safety margin for repeated skeletal-muscle activation. Sensory pathways are not the target.
17. Which MG manifestation requires emergency assessment?
Respiratory weakness or severe bulbar weakness suggests myasthenic crisis and risk of ventilation or airway-protection failure.
18. How does pyridostigmine improve MG symptoms?
It inhibits acetylcholinesterase, leaving more acetylcholine available to stimulate the reduced number of functional postsynaptic receptors.
19. What is the central lesion in Parkinson disease?
Degeneration of dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra pars compacta depletes nigrostriatal dopamine and disrupts basal-ganglia motor control.
20. How does essential tremor differ from Parkinson tremor?
Essential tremor is usually bilateral action/postural tremor and commonly involves the head or voice; Parkinson tremor is usually asymmetric at onset, occurs at rest, and accompanies bradykinesia or rigidity.
21. What handwriting clue separates Parkinson disease from essential tremor?
Parkinson disease often causes micrographia—small, cramped writing—whereas essential tremor makes writing shaky but usually not abnormally small.
