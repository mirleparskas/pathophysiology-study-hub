Assigned Reading Notes

Reading scopeThese notes cover the assigned textbook readings and the IBD paper. They include background concepts that may not be named directly in the learning outcomes but are in the assigned reading sections.

McCance 9th ed. pp. 1318-1324, 1340, 1390: Constipation, Diarrhea, IBS

  • Constipation is difficult, infrequent, incomplete, or painful stool passage; normal bowel frequency varies widely, so change from baseline matters.
  • Primary constipation includes functional evacuation disorder, slow-transit constipation, and constipation-predominant IBS.
  • Secondary constipation can result from low fiber/fluid intake, immobility, medications, opioids, neurologic disease, endocrine/metabolic disease, pregnancy, pain, obstruction, strictures, fissures, hemorrhoids, or colorectal cancer.
  • Slow transit allows excessive water reabsorption, producing harder stool and more difficult evacuation.
  • Fecal impaction can paradoxically cause low-volume leakage diarrhea as fluid/mucus passes around impacted stool.
  • Diarrhea is acute, persistent, or chronic loose/watery stool; major risks are dehydration, electrolyte loss, metabolic acidosis, weight loss, and shock in vulnerable patients.
  • Large-volume diarrhea usually reflects excess luminal water or secretion; small-volume diarrhea often reflects inflammatory disease, urgency, tenesmus, or distal colon/rectal involvement.
  • IBS is a disorder of gut-brain interaction with abdominal pain plus altered bowel habits, without structural disease explaining symptoms.
  • IBS mechanisms are multifactorial: visceral hypersensitivity, altered motility, brain-gut dysregulation, microbiome changes, postinfectious immune activation, stress response, food triggers, and altered serotonin signalling.
  • IBS manifestations include recurrent abdominal pain, bloating, change in stool frequency/form, mucus, urgency, and subtypes with constipation, diarrhea, mixed, or unclassified patterns.

McCance 9th ed. pp. 1324-1326, 1381: GERD and Hiatal Hernia

  • GERD occurs when gastric contents reflux into the esophagus often enough to cause symptoms, mucosal injury, or complications.
  • Key mechanisms include transient lower esophageal sphincter relaxation, low LES tone, delayed gastric emptying, impaired esophageal clearance, reduced salivary neutralization, increased intra-abdominal pressure, and hiatal hernia.
  • Acid, pepsin, and sometimes bile irritate squamous epithelium, causing inflammation, erosions, strictures, ulceration, bleeding, or Barrett esophagus.
  • Typical manifestations: heartburn, regurgitation, sour/bitter taste, dysphagia, epigastric/substernal discomfort, cough, hoarseness, wheeze, dental erosion, or sleep disruption.
  • Sliding hiatal hernia occurs when the gastroesophageal junction and part of the stomach slide above the diaphragm; it is common and strongly associated with reflux.
  • Paraesophageal hiatal hernia occurs when part of the stomach herniates beside the esophagus while the gastroesophageal junction may remain in place; reflux may be less prominent, but obstruction, volvulus, ischemia, or strangulation is the major concern.

McCance 9th ed. pp. 1328-1333: Gastritis and Peptic Ulcer Disease

  • Gastritis is inflammation of the gastric mucosa; it may be acute, chronic, erosive, autoimmune, H. pylori related, or chemical/reactive.
  • Acute gastritis usually reflects mucosal barrier injury from NSAIDs, alcohol, severe physiologic stress, bile reflux, toxins, infection, or uremia.
  • NSAIDs reduce prostaglandin-mediated mucus, bicarbonate, blood flow, and epithelial repair, making acid-pepsin injury more likely.
  • H. pylori uses urease, motility, adhesins, toxins, and inflammatory signalling to survive in the stomach and injure mucosa.
  • Chronic autoimmune fundal gastritis damages parietal cells, reducing acid and intrinsic factor and increasing risk for pernicious anemia and gastric cancer.
  • Peptic ulcer disease is a mucosal break extending through the muscularis mucosa, usually from H. pylori, NSAIDs, or acid hypersecretion.
  • Duodenal ulcers often have increased acid load and pain that occurs 2 to 3 hours after meals or at night and improves with food or antacids.
  • Gastric ulcers often reflect impaired mucosal defense and can worsen with food; malignancy must be considered when evaluating gastric ulcer lesions.
  • Complications include bleeding, perforation, penetration, gastric outlet obstruction, anemia, and peritonitis.

McCance 9th ed. pp. 1336-1341 and IBD Paper: IBD, Diverticular Disease, Appendicitis

  • IBD is chronic relapsing intestinal inflammation driven by genetic susceptibility, dysregulated mucosal immunity, epithelial barrier dysfunction, microbiome changes, and environmental triggers.
  • Crohn disease can affect any part of the GI tract, commonly terminal ileum and colon, with skip lesions and transmural inflammation.
  • Ulcerative colitis begins in the rectum and extends proximally in a continuous pattern; inflammation is usually limited to mucosa/submucosa.
  • Transmural Crohn inflammation predisposes to strictures, fistulas, abscesses, obstruction, and malabsorption.
  • UC predisposes to bloody diarrhea, urgency, tenesmus, toxic megacolon, and colorectal cancer risk with long-standing extensive disease.
  • Diverticulosis is the presence of mucosal outpouchings through weak points in the colon wall, commonly associated with increased intraluminal pressure and aging.
  • Diverticulitis occurs when diverticula become inflamed or microperforate, causing localized pain, fever, leukocytosis, altered bowel habits, abscess, perforation, fistula, or obstruction.
  • Appendicitis usually begins with luminal obstruction by fecalith, lymphoid hyperplasia, parasite, tumor, or foreign material, followed by bacterial overgrowth, distention, ischemia, inflammation, and possible perforation.

McCance 9th ed. pp. 887-888, 1347-1353, 1392-1393: Hepatitis

  • Hepatitis means liver inflammation; viral hepatitis can range from asymptomatic infection to acute liver failure, chronic hepatitis, cirrhosis, and hepatocellular carcinoma.
  • The typical acute clinical course includes incubation, prodromal/preicteric symptoms, icteric phase, and recovery/convalescence.
  • Prodromal symptoms: fatigue, malaise, anorexia, nausea, vomiting, low-grade fever, myalgias, right upper quadrant discomfort, and aversion to smoking or fatty foods.
  • Icteric phase: jaundice, dark urine, pale stools, pruritus, hepatomegaly, and elevated bilirubin/liver enzymes.
  • Fulminant hepatitis can cause acute liver failure, coagulopathy, encephalopathy, hypoglycemia, renal dysfunction, and death.
  • Chronic HBV, HCV, and HBV/HDV infection can persist beyond 6 months and progress to cirrhosis and hepatocellular carcinoma.
  • Autoimmune hepatitis is T-cell mediated liver injury in genetically susceptible individuals and may present with jaundice, fatigue, anorexia, amenorrhea, or acute liver failure.

McCance 9th ed. pp. 1353-1357: Gallbladder and Pancreatic Disorders

  • Cholelithiasis is gallstone formation from supersaturated bile, impaired cholesterol/bilirubin/bile acid handling, mucin nucleation, and gallbladder hypomotility or stasis.
  • Risk factors include female sex, pregnancy, estrogen exposure, obesity, rapid weight loss, fasting/TPN, genetics, age, ileal disease or resection, Crohn disease, cirrhosis, and hemolysis.
  • Stones may remain silent or obstruct the cystic/common bile duct, causing biliary colic, right upper quadrant or epigastric pain, fatty food intolerance, nausea, vomiting, and jaundice if common duct obstruction occurs.
  • Cholecystitis is gallbladder inflammation, usually after cystic duct obstruction; distention and pressure impair blood flow and can cause ischemia, necrosis, perforation, fever, leukocytosis, guarding, and persistent RUQ pain.
  • Acute pancreatitis is autodigestion and inflammation of the pancreas after premature activation of pancreatic enzymes inside pancreatic tissue.
  • Major causes include gallstones, alcohol use, hypertriglyceridemia, medications, trauma, infection, procedures, and hypercalcemia.
  • Manifestations include severe epigastric pain radiating to the back, nausea/vomiting, fever, tachycardia, ileus, hypovolemia, hypocalcemia, shock, ARDS, renal injury, and pseudocyst/necrosis in severe disease.
  • Chronic pancreatitis is progressive irreversible fibrosis and loss of exocrine/endocrine function, commonly linked to long-term alcohol use, recurrent acute pancreatitis, genetic disease, obstruction, or autoimmune disease.
  • Chronic pancreatitis manifestations include persistent/recurrent pain, malabsorption, steatorrhea, weight loss, fat-soluble vitamin deficiency, and diabetes.

McCance 9th ed. pp. 179, 1357-1362: GI Cancers, Colon and Rectum Focus

  • Colorectal cancer usually develops through stepwise genetic and epigenetic changes that convert normal mucosa to adenoma and then carcinoma.
  • Important pathways include chromosomal instability/APC pathway, microsatellite instability from mismatch repair defects, and serrated/CpG methylation pathway.
  • Risk factors include age, adenomatous polyps, family history, Lynch syndrome, familial adenomatous polyposis, long-standing IBD, low-fiber/high processed meat diet patterns, obesity, inactivity, smoking, alcohol, and type 2 diabetes.
  • Right-sided colon cancer may present with occult bleeding, iron-deficiency anemia, fatigue, vague discomfort, or late obstruction.
  • Left-sided/rectal cancer more often causes change in bowel habits, narrowed stool caliber, visible blood, tenesmus, cramping, or obstruction.
  • Staging depends on depth of invasion, lymph node involvement, and metastasis; liver metastasis is common because portal drainage carries tumor cells to the liver.

Lecture Transcript Pearls

Module 5 lecture add-inUse these as instructor-emphasis notes from the cleaned GI disorders transcript. The big clinical thread is pattern recognition plus red-flag screening: GI symptoms overlap with cardiac, renal, liver, medication, cancer, and referred-pain presentations.

GI Dysfunction Patterns

  • Common manifestations include anorexia, nausea, retching, vomiting, constipation, diarrhea, abdominal pain, and GI bleeding.
  • Anorexia is loss of desire to eat despite physiologic hunger signals; consequences can include weight loss, protein-energy malnutrition, sarcopenia, functional decline, and cachexia.
  • Nausea is subjective; retching is muscular vomiting activity without expulsion; vomiting can quickly produce fluid, electrolyte, and acid-base disturbances.
  • Treat vomiting by assessing the cause and replacing losses, not only suppressing the symptom.
  • Abdominal pain may be difficult to localize and can reflect referred pain or non-GI disease, including cardiac causes of epigastric pain.

Constipation and Medication Triggers

  • Primary constipation includes functional defecation disorder, slow-transit constipation, and constipation-predominant IBS.
  • Secondary constipation can come from diet, dehydration, medications, neurologic disease, structural disease, or suppression of the urge to defecate.
  • Medication causes emphasized in lecture: calcium carbonate or aluminum antacids, anticholinergics, iron, bismuth, opioids, and GLP-1 agonists.
  • Opioids slow transit through mu-opioid receptor activation in the gut.
  • Straining can engorge hemorrhoidal veins, causing rectal pain, bleeding, itching, or thrombosis.

Diverticular Disease

  • Diverticulosis: pouch-like mucosal herniations through weak colon wall points.
  • Often asymptomatic; may cause bleeding or vague bowel changes.
  • Diverticulitis: inflammation/microperforation with LLQ pain, fever, leukocytosis, nausea, constipation or diarrhea.
  • Complications: abscess, perforation, peritonitis, fistula, obstruction, sepsis.

Appendicitis

  • Obstruction increases intraluminal pressure and bacterial overgrowth.
  • Venous congestion and ischemia worsen inflammation.
  • Pain often starts periumbilical and migrates to right lower quadrant as parietal peritoneum becomes involved.
  • Perforation risk increases with delayed diagnosis.

Student Clinical Consult Add-Ins

How to use theseThese presentations add applied clinical framing to the GERD, gastritis, and peptic ulcer disease readings. Slide visuals were not added.

GERD Clinical Consult

  • GERD is retrograde movement of gastric contents, including acid, pepsin, and bile salts, into the esophagus, causing symptoms or complications.
  • Core mechanisms: weak or transiently relaxed LES, impaired esophageal clearance, delayed gastric emptying, sliding hiatal hernia, increased intra-abdominal pressure, smoking, obesity, pregnancy, alcohol/diet triggers, and medications.
  • Typical symptoms: heartburn, acid regurgitation, chest discomfort, bloating, belching, and dysphagia if complicated.
  • Atypical symptoms: chronic cough, asthma-like symptoms, hoarseness, throat symptoms, and sleep disruption.
  • Complications: erosive esophagitis, strictures, bleeding, Barrett esophagus, and esophageal adenocarcinoma risk.
  • H. pylori is more directly linked with gastritis, PUD, and gastric adenocarcinoma than with classic GERD; its relationship with GERD is complex and not a simple cause-effect pattern.

Long-Term NSAID Use

  • NSAIDs inhibit cyclooxygenase enzymes. COX-2 inhibition reduces inflammation and pain; COX-1 inhibition reduces protective gastric prostaglandins.
  • Reduced prostaglandins lower mucus, bicarbonate, mucosal blood flow, epithelial repair, tight-junction integrity, and platelet support.
  • Loss of mucosal defense allows acid and pepsin to injure the stomach and duodenum, causing gastritis, erosions, ulcers, bleeding, perforation, and peritonitis.
  • NSAID enteropathy can affect the small bowel, causing erosions, ulcers, occult bleeding, iron deficiency, protein-losing enteropathy, and diaphragm-like strictures.
  • High-risk features: age over 65, H. pylori, prior ulcer or GI bleed, high-dose or multiple NSAIDs, steroids, anticoagulants, antiplatelets, alcohol, smoking, chronic illness, and comorbidity burden.

Clinical Practice Quick Cards

GI History

  • Onset, location, radiation, character, timing with meals/stool, triggers, severity, duration.
  • Stool frequency, form, blood/mucus, melena, steatorrhea, nocturnal symptoms, urgency, tenesmus.
  • Meds: NSAIDs, antibiotics, opioids, iron, anticholinergics, PPIs, anticoagulants, supplements.
  • Travel, sick contacts, food exposures, alcohol, cannabis, sexual/blood exposure, pregnancy possibility.

Red Flags

  • GI bleeding, black stool, hematemesis, unexplained anemia, jaundice, persistent vomiting.
  • Fever with severe abdominal pain, guarding, rebound, rigid abdomen, syncope, hypotension.
  • Unintentional weight loss, nocturnal diarrhea, new bowel change over age 50, family cancer history.
  • Severe dehydration, oliguria, confusion, immunocompromised state, pregnancy, infant/older adult frailty.

Focused Assessment

  • Vitals, hydration, orthostatic symptoms, mental status, pain behaviour.
  • Inspect abdomen, auscultate, palpate gently, assess peritoneal signs, masses, organomegaly.
  • Skin/sclera for jaundice, pallor, bruising, spider angiomas, scratch marks, edema.
  • Consider rectal exam, stool testing, labs, pregnancy test, imaging, endoscopy/colonoscopy depending on presentation.

Practice and Active Recall

1. How can fasting help differentiate osmotic from secretory diarrhea?
Osmotic diarrhea improves or stops when the nonabsorbed solute is removed; secretory diarrhea usually persists despite fasting.
2. Why can fecal impaction cause diarrhea?
Fluid and mucus can leak around impacted stool, producing low-volume overflow diarrhea.
3. What is the core pathophysiology of GERD?
Failure of anti-reflux barriers allows acid/pepsin or bile exposure of esophageal mucosa, producing symptoms and possible mucosal injury.
4. Why do NSAIDs increase gastritis and ulcer risk?
They reduce prostaglandins, weakening mucus, bicarbonate secretion, mucosal blood flow, and repair.
5. Crohn vs UC: what is the fastest comparison?
Crohn is transmural, skip-lesion disease anywhere in the GI tract; UC is continuous mucosal disease starting in the rectum and limited to colon/rectum.
6. What makes appendicitis dangerous if untreated?
Luminal obstruction can progress to ischemia, necrosis, perforation, abscess, peritonitis, and sepsis.
7. Which hepatitis viruses commonly become chronic?
HBV, HCV, and HDV with HBV can become chronic; HAV does not cause chronic infection.
8. How do gallstones cause cholecystitis?
A stone lodges in the cystic duct, causing obstruction, gallbladder distention, inflammation, reduced blood flow, and possible ischemia/perforation.
9. Why does acute pancreatitis cause systemic illness?
Activated pancreatic enzymes and inflammation injure pancreatic and surrounding tissue, causing third spacing, hypovolemia, cytokine release, organ dysfunction, and shock in severe disease.
10. Why does colon cancer often metastasize to the liver?
Venous drainage from much of the colon travels through the portal circulation to the liver.
11. What is the difference between nausea, retching, and vomiting?
Nausea is the subjective unpleasant sensation; retching is the muscular vomiting sequence without expulsion; vomiting is expulsion of gastric contents and can cause fluid, electrolyte, and acid-base disturbances.
12. Why should epigastric pain not automatically be treated as stomach pain?
Epigastric pain can overlap with referred pain or non-GI disease, including cardiac causes, so history, red flags, and diagnostic context matter.
13. Which medication classes were emphasized as constipation triggers in lecture?
Calcium carbonate or aluminum antacids, anticholinergics, iron, bismuth, opioids, and GLP-1 agonists were highlighted.
14. Why do opioids commonly cause constipation?
They activate mu-opioid receptors in the gut, slowing intestinal transit and promoting harder stool.
15. Why is anorexia clinically important beyond reduced appetite?
It can contribute to weight loss, protein-energy malnutrition, sarcopenia, functional decline, and cachexia, especially in chronic disease or cancer.
