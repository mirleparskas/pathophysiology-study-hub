McCance 9th ed. pp. 1153-1158: Clinical Manifestations of Pulmonary Alterations

  • Dyspnea is a subjective sense of breathing discomfort caused by mismatch between respiratory drive and the mechanical ability to ventilate.
  • Dyspnea can come from hypoxemia, increased work of breathing, decreased lung compliance, airway obstruction, pneumonia, PE, pulmonary edema, pleural disease, anemia, anxiety, pain, or heart disease.
  • Severe dyspnea may show accessory muscle use, nasal flaring, retractions, inability to speak full sentences, agitation, or fatigue.
  • Hypoventilation means inadequate alveolar ventilation for metabolic demand; PaCO2 rises, causing hypercapnia and respiratory acidosis.
  • Hyperventilation means alveolar ventilation exceeds metabolic demand; PaCO2 falls, causing hypocapnia and respiratory alkalosis.
  • Cyanosis is bluish skin/mucosa from increased deoxygenated hemoglobin; it is an insensitive late sign and can be absent in anemia or carbon monoxide poisoning.
  • Hypoxemia is reduced arterial oxygenation; hypoxia is inadequate tissue oxygenation. Hypoxemia can cause confusion, tachycardia, cyanosis, edema, oliguria, and organ injury.
  • Acute respiratory failure is inadequate gas exchange from hypoxemia, hypercapnia, or both; it may require oxygenation support and ventilatory support.

McCance 9th ed. pp. 1176-1179: Respiratory Tract Infections

  • Pneumonia is infection and inflammation of terminal airways and alveoli, leading to exudate, consolidation, impaired diffusion, V/Q mismatch, and hypoxemia.
  • Common pneumonia pathogens include Streptococcus pneumoniae, Haemophilus influenzae, Staphylococcus aureus, gram-negative organisms, atypical bacteria such as Mycoplasma, and respiratory viruses.
  • Bacterial pneumonia commonly follows aspiration of colonized oropharyngeal secretions, impaired clearance, viral injury, smoking, chronic lung disease, immunosuppression, or altered consciousness.
  • Clinical manifestations include fever, chills, cough, purulent sputum, pleuritic chest pain, crackles/bronchial breath sounds, dyspnea, tachypnea, hypoxemia, and malaise.
  • Acute bronchitis is acute inflammation of the tracheobronchial tree, usually viral, causing cough with or without sputum, chest discomfort, wheeze, and low-grade systemic symptoms.
  • TB is airborne, macrophage-associated, granulomatous infection that may remain latent or reactivate when immune containment fails.

McCance 9th ed. pp. 1205-1207, 1383: Cystic Fibrosis

  • CF is autosomal recessive, caused by pathogenic CFTR variants affecting chloride and sodium/water movement across epithelial surfaces.
  • Defective CFTR causes dehydrated, thick secretions in airways, pancreas, hepatobiliary tract, sweat glands, intestine, and reproductive tract.
  • The core triad is obstruction, infection, and inflammation.
  • Respiratory disease includes mucus plugging, impaired mucociliary clearance, chronic bacterial infection, bronchiectasis, air trapping, hypoxemia, and progressive respiratory failure.
  • Non-respiratory manifestations include salty sweat, meconium ileus, pancreatic insufficiency, malabsorption, steatorrhea, poor growth, fat-soluble vitamin deficiency, distal intestinal obstruction syndrome, liver disease, infertility, and CF-related diabetes.

McCance 9th ed. pp. 1167-1174, 1203-1204: COPD and Asthma

  • COPD is persistent, usually progressive airflow limitation associated with chronic inflammatory response to noxious particles or gases, especially cigarette smoke.
  • Chronic bronchitis is airway-predominant COPD with mucus gland enlargement, goblet-cell hyperplasia, chronic productive cough, airway inflammation, and mucus plugging.
  • Emphysema is alveolar wall destruction with enlarged airspaces, loss of elastic recoil, small airway collapse, air trapping, hyperinflation, and reduced gas exchange surface.
  • COPD manifestations include chronic cough, sputum, exertional dyspnea, wheeze, prolonged expiration, barrel chest, accessory muscle use, fatigue, weight loss, hypoxemia, hypercapnia, pulmonary hypertension, and cor pulmonale.
  • Asthma is chronic airway inflammation with bronchial hyperresponsiveness and usually reversible airflow obstruction.
  • Asthma mechanisms include IgE/mast-cell activation in allergic asthma, eosinophilic inflammation, cytokines, mucus production, airway edema, bronchoconstriction, and airway remodeling in chronic disease.
  • Asthma manifestations include episodic wheeze, cough, chest tightness, dyspnea, prolonged expiration, nocturnal/exercise symptoms, and acute exacerbations with severe airflow obstruction.

McCance 9th ed. pp. 1179-1181: Pulmonary Vascular Disease

  • Pulmonary embolism is obstruction of pulmonary arterial flow, usually from thromboembolism that originates in deep veins.
  • PE causes high V/Q mismatch or dead space, increased pulmonary vascular resistance, hypoxemia, pleuritic pain, dyspnea, tachycardia, and possible right ventricular strain or shock.
  • Pulmonary artery hypertension is sustained high pressure in pulmonary arteries from vascular remodeling, vasoconstriction, thrombosis, hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction, left heart disease, lung disease, or chronic thromboembolic disease.
  • Cor pulmonale is right ventricular hypertrophy/dilation and failure caused by pulmonary hypertension from lung or pulmonary vascular disease.

McCance 9th ed. pp. 1163, 1191-1194: Pediatric Respiratory Infections and Obstruction

  • Croup is upper airway obstruction from laryngotracheobronchitis, usually viral, causing subglottic edema, barking cough, hoarseness, stridor, and retractions if severe.
  • Acute epiglottitis is a life-threatening supraglottic infection causing rapid airway obstruction, drooling, dysphagia, fever, muffled voice, tripod positioning, and respiratory distress.
  • Tonsillitis may be viral or bacterial, including group A streptococcal infection; inflammation and hypertrophy can narrow the upper airway.
  • Bronchiolitis is usually RSV-related small airway infection in children under 2, causing epithelial necrosis, edema, mucus/debris plugging, air trapping, wheeze, tachypnea, retractions, and feeding difficulty.
  • Pediatric pneumonia can be viral, bacterial, or atypical; children often present with cough, fever, tachypnea, wheeze/crackles, retractions, nasal flaring, dehydration, and vomiting.

McCance 9th ed. pp. 1182-1185: Lung Cancer

  • Lung cancer develops through accumulated genetic/epigenetic injury from carcinogens, chronic inflammation, impaired repair, and dysregulated cell growth.
  • Major risk factors include cigarette smoking, second-hand smoke, radon, asbestos, occupational exposures, air pollution, prior radiation, family history, and chronic lung disease.
  • Non-small cell lung cancer includes adenocarcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma, and large cell carcinoma; it generally grows/spreads less rapidly than small cell lung cancer.
  • Large cell carcinoma is an undifferentiated NSCLC subtype, often peripheral, aggressive, and associated with worse prognosis than some other NSCLC subtypes.
  • Small cell lung cancer is strongly smoking-associated, neuroendocrine, rapidly growing, early metastatic, often initially chemo/radiation responsive, and has poor overall prognosis.
  • Manifestations may include cough, hemoptysis, dyspnea, chest pain, recurrent pneumonia, weight loss, fatigue, hoarseness, superior vena cava syndrome, bone pain, neurologic symptoms, or paraneoplastic syndromes.

2. Respiratory Infections

Pneumonia

  • Alveolar infection and inflammation fill airspaces with exudate.
  • Common causes: S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae, S. aureus, gram-negative rods, atypical bacteria, influenza, RSV, SARS-CoV-2, and other respiratory viruses.
  • Risk factors: age extremes, smoking, COPD, aspiration, impaired cough/swallow, altered consciousness, immunosuppression, viral infection, hospitalization, ventilation.
  • Manifestations: cough, sputum, fever, chills, pleuritic pain, crackles, bronchial breath sounds, dyspnea, tachypnea, hypoxemia, fatigue.

Acute Bronchitis

  • Acute inflammation of bronchi, usually viral.
  • Airway epithelial irritation increases cough receptor stimulation and mucus production.
  • Manifestations: cough lasting days to weeks, sputum possible, wheeze, chest soreness, low-grade fever, URI symptoms.
  • Pneumonia becomes a concern with focal findings, hypoxemia, high fever, tachypnea, or systemic toxicity.

5. Pulmonary Vascular Disease

Pulmonary Embolism

  • Usually thrombus from leg/pelvic veins travels to pulmonary artery.
  • Blocks perfusion to ventilated alveoli, increasing dead space.
  • Causes dyspnea, pleuritic chest pain, tachycardia, hypoxemia, hemoptysis, syncope, or shock.

Pulmonary Hypertension

  • Increased pulmonary artery pressure from vasoconstriction, remodeling, thrombosis, hypoxia, lung disease, left heart disease, or chronic emboli.
  • Raises right ventricular afterload.
  • Symptoms include exertional dyspnea, fatigue, chest pain, syncope, edema.

Cor Pulmonale

  • Right ventricular hypertrophy/dilation/failure caused by pulmonary disease or pulmonary hypertension.
  • Findings: JVD, peripheral edema, hepatomegaly, ascites, loud P2, worsening hypoxemia.
  • COPD and chronic hypoxic lung disease are common drivers.

Student Clinical Consult Add-Ins

Tuberculosis in Canada

  • M. tuberculosis is slow-growing, acid-fast, aerobic, lipid-rich, and intracellular.
  • Airborne transmission and upper-lobe cavitation make active pulmonary TB a public-health issue.
  • Major risk factors include HIV, TNF-alpha inhibitors, diabetes, overcrowding, homelessness, incarceration, healthcare exposure, and birth/travel in endemic regions.
  • Diagnosis: TST/IGRA show sensitization; suspected active disease needs chest imaging, AFB smear, NAAT/PCR, and culture.
  • Drug-sensitive active TB is treated with multidrug therapy; latent TB treatment prevents reactivation.

Streptococcus pneumoniae

  • Gram-positive, lancet-shaped bacterium causing otitis, sinusitis, pneumonia, bacteremia, and meningitis.
  • Capsule prevents opsonization and phagocytosis by neutrophils/macrophages.
  • Alveolar invasion causes macrophage cytokines, neutrophil recruitment, exudate, consolidation, and hypoxemia.
  • Adults may show fever, productive cough, pleuritic chest pain; older adults may show confusion, decreased alertness, or hypothermia.
  • Prevention includes vaccination, smoking cessation, hygiene, and chronic disease optimization. Check current provincial schedule before counselling.

Clinical Practice Quick Cards

Respiratory Assessment

  • Vitals, SpO2, work of breathing, speech, accessory muscle use, mental status, hydration, perfusion.
  • Ask onset, cough/sputum, hemoptysis, wheeze, chest pain, fever, exposures, smoking/vaping, travel, immune status.
  • Auscultate for wheeze, crackles, decreased breath sounds, bronchial sounds, stridor, pleural rub.

Urgent Red Flags

  • Severe dyspnea, cyanosis, altered LOC, exhaustion, silent chest, stridor, hypotension, SpO2 not responding to oxygen.
  • Hemoptysis, suspected PE, suspected active TB, sepsis signs, severe asthma/COPD exacerbation, pediatric drooling/tripod.
  • ABG/capnography matters when hypoventilation or hypercapnia is suspected.

Treatment Logic

  • Hypoxemia needs oxygenation support; hypercapnia from hypoventilation needs ventilation support.
  • Obstruction: bronchodilation, anti-inflammatory therapy, trigger control, airway clearance as appropriate.
  • Infection: identify severity, likely organism, isolation needs, cultures/testing, antimicrobials when bacterial/TB likely.

Practice and Active Recall

1. Hypoventilation causes which blood gas pattern?
Increased PaCO2, respiratory acidosis, and possible secondary hypoxemia.
2. What is the most common mechanism of hypoxemia?
Ventilation-perfusion mismatch.
3. Why does pneumonia cause hypoxemia?
Inflammatory exudate fills alveoli, causing low V/Q or shunt physiology and impaired diffusion.
4. What immune response contains TB?
Th1-mediated cell-mediated immunity with IFN-gamma/TNF-alpha macrophage activation and granuloma formation.
5. CF core triad?
Obstruction, infection, and inflammation from thick dehydrated secretions.
6. Emphysema vs chronic bronchitis?
Emphysema is alveolar destruction/loss of elastic recoil; chronic bronchitis is mucus hypersecretion and chronic productive cough from airway inflammation.
7. Asthma pathophysiology in one sentence?
Chronic airway inflammation causes bronchial hyperresponsiveness, reversible bronchoconstriction, mucus, edema, and sometimes remodeling.
8. Why can PE cause sudden dyspnea with clear lungs?
Perfusion is blocked while ventilation remains, creating dead space and acute V/Q mismatch.
9. Why is epiglottitis an emergency?
Rapid supraglottic swelling can obstruct the airway; agitation or throat exam can worsen obstruction.
10. Which lung cancer has the strongest early metastatic tendency?
Small cell lung cancer.
