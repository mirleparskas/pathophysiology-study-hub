Assigned Reading Notes

Reading scopeThese notes cover the assigned textbook readings and supporting paper, including background mechanisms that may not be named directly in the learning outcomes.

McCance 9th ed. pp. 50-53: Free Radicals and ROS

  • Free radicals have unpaired electrons and are highly reactive; reactive oxygen species can damage lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids.
  • Major ROS sources include mitochondria, inflammation, toxins, radiation, ischemia-reperfusion, and oxidative metabolism.
  • Oxidative stress occurs when ROS production exceeds antioxidant defenses.
  • ROS injure cell membranes through lipid peroxidation, alter enzymes/receptors through protein oxidation, and damage DNA.
  • In cardiovascular disease, ROS oxidize LDL, injure endothelium, reduce nitric oxide availability, worsen vasoconstriction, and contribute to reperfusion injury.

McCance 9th ed. pp. 85-91: Cellular Death

  • Reversible injury becomes irreversible when ATP depletion, mitochondrial dysfunction, calcium overload, membrane injury, and DNA/protein damage cannot be corrected.
  • Necrosis is uncontrolled injury-related death with membrane rupture and inflammation.
  • Apoptosis is regulated programmed death with cell shrinkage, fragmentation, and minimal inflammation when fragments are cleared.
  • Myocardial infarction is classically associated with necrosis of cardiomyocytes, which releases troponin.
  • Apoptosis can contribute to remodeling and border-zone injury but is not the main acute infarct-core pattern.

McCance 9th ed. pp. 174-175, 1069-1084: Atherosclerosis, Coronary Heart Disease, ACS/MI

  • Atherosclerosis is chronic inflammatory plaque disease of medium and large arteries.
  • Endothelial dysfunction allows LDL entry, monocyte adhesion, inflammation, and platelet interaction.
  • Oxidized LDL drives macrophage uptake and foam-cell formation.
  • Fatty streaks can progress to fibrous plaques as smooth muscle migrates/proliferates and deposits extracellular matrix.
  • Stable plaques often cause predictable flow limitation; vulnerable plaques are rupture-prone because of thin caps, large lipid cores, and inflammatory enzymes.
  • Plaque rupture exposes thrombogenic material, causing platelet adhesion, coagulation, thrombus formation, and acute occlusion.
  • CAD occurs when coronary supply cannot meet myocardial oxygen demand.
  • Ischemia first impairs contractility and electrical stability; prolonged ischemia causes infarction.
  • ACS includes unstable angina, NSTEMI, and STEMI; troponin elevation distinguishes MI from unstable angina.
  • MI healing replaces necrotic myocardium with scar, reducing contractile reserve and increasing remodeling/HF risk.

McCance 9th ed. pp. 1061-1066: Hypertension

  • Blood pressure rises through increased cardiac output, increased systemic vascular resistance, increased blood volume, arterial stiffness, or combinations of these.
  • Primary hypertension is multifactorial and includes genetic susceptibility, SNS activity, RAAS activity, renal sodium handling, endothelial dysfunction, insulin resistance, inflammation, and vascular remodeling.
  • Secondary hypertension has an identifiable driver such as renal disease, renal artery stenosis, endocrine disease, sleep apnea, medications, or vascular disease.
  • Chronic hypertension injures endothelium, accelerates atherosclerosis, increases afterload, and causes arteriolar remodeling.
  • Target organs include heart, brain, kidneys, retina, and peripheral vasculature.

McCance 9th ed. pp. 978, 1073: DVT and PAD

  • DVT is venous thrombus formation, usually in deep lower-extremity veins.
  • Virchow triad explains DVT risk: venous stasis, endothelial injury, and hypercoagulability.
  • DVT can obstruct venous return locally and can embolize to the lungs as pulmonary embolism.
  • PAD is usually atherosclerotic narrowing of arteries supplying the extremities.
  • PAD causes exertional muscle ischemia, classically intermittent claudication, and severe disease causes rest pain, ulcers, gangrene, and tissue loss.

McCance 9th ed. pp. 1096-1101: Heart Failure and Dysrhythmias

  • Heart failure is a clinical syndrome where cardiac output is inadequate, filling pressures are elevated, or both.
  • Left-sided HF causes pulmonary congestion and reduced systemic perfusion.
  • Right-sided HF causes systemic venous congestion and may follow left-sided HF or pulmonary hypertension/lung disease.
  • HFrEF/systolic HF is impaired contraction, often from MI/remodeling/cardiomyopathy.
  • HFpEF/diastolic HF is impaired relaxation/filling with a stiff ventricle, often linked with hypertension, aging, obesity, diabetes, and LV hypertrophy.
  • Compensatory SNS and RAAS activation initially supports perfusion but chronically worsens afterload, preload, remodeling, fibrosis, and fluid retention.
  • Dysrhythmias can worsen HF by reducing filling time, reducing coordinated contraction, or causing sudden loss of output.

McCance 9th ed. pp. 1109-1119: Congenital Heart Disease

  • Congenital lesions alter pressure, flow, oxygenation, and workload through abnormal valves, vessels, septa, or shunts.
  • Left-to-right shunts increase pulmonary blood flow and can eventually cause pulmonary hypertension and HF.
  • Right-to-left shunts allow deoxygenated blood into systemic circulation, causing cyanosis.
  • Obstructive/right-sided lesions can limit pulmonary blood flow and cause hypoxemia.
  • Clinical manifestations depend on shunt direction, degree of obstruction, pulmonary vascular resistance, and compensatory changes.

CORDIOPREV Trial: Mediterranean Diet vs Low-Fat Diet

  • CORDIOPREV was a randomized trial in patients with established coronary heart disease comparing Mediterranean and low-fat dietary interventions over long-term follow-up.
  • The Mediterranean diet was superior to the low-fat diet for secondary prevention of major cardiovascular events, with a reported hazard-ratio decrease of about 26% in the ScienceDirect summary.
  • Mechanism connection: Mediterranean-style eating can support better lipid profiles, endothelial function, insulin sensitivity, inflammatory tone, oxidative stress balance, and cardiometabolic risk.
  • Clinical takeaway: diet is part of secondary prevention after CAD/MI, not just general wellness advice.

2. CAD, Myocardial Ischemia, and ACS

Ischemic Cell Injury

  1. Coronary flow or oxygen content falls below myocardial demand.
  2. Mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation falls; ATP drops.
  3. Ion pumps fail; sodium/water enter cells; potassium gradients fail.
  4. Anaerobic metabolism increases lactic acid and lowers pH.
  5. Calcium overload activates damaging enzymes and worsens mitochondrial injury.
  6. ROS and reperfusion injury can damage membranes, proteins, DNA, and microvasculature.
  7. Prolonged severe injury causes necrosis and troponin release.

3. Hypertension

Primary Hypertension

  • No single identifiable cause; most cases.
  • Genetics plus environment alter SNS tone, RAAS, renal sodium retention, endothelial function, insulin resistance, inflammation, and vascular remodeling.
  • Final pathways: increased volume/output, increased SVR, and arterial stiffness.
  • Chronic pressure causes endothelial injury, LVH, nephrosclerosis, retinopathy, stroke risk, and accelerated atherosclerosis.

Secondary Hypertension

  • Identifiable disease, medication, endocrine problem, renal problem, sleep apnea, or vascular cause.
  • Consider when HTN is abrupt, severe, young onset, resistant, associated with hypokalemia, renal bruit, episodic symptoms, or medication trigger.
  • May improve if the cause is treated before permanent vascular remodeling occurs.

Student Clinical Consult Add-Ins

How to use theseThese presentations add clinical framing to the textbook readings. They are summarized here as mechanism-based notes and practice links, not as slide galleries.

Hypertension and ACE Inhibitors

  • Hypertension reflects sustained elevation in pressure from increased cardiac output, increased peripheral vascular resistance, increased blood volume, arterial stiffness, or combined mechanisms.
  • Primary hypertension is usually gradual and multifactorial; secondary hypertension has an identifiable driver and may improve when the cause is treated.
  • Long-term regulation depends heavily on kidneys and RAAS, with sympathetic tone and endothelial function shaping vascular resistance.
  • Target-organ damage: LV hypertrophy/heart failure, stroke/TIA, CKD/proteinuria, retinopathy, endothelial injury, and accelerated atherosclerosis.
  • Risk factors to assess: age, genetics, ethnicity, obesity, high sodium intake, inactivity, alcohol, smoking, chronic stress, diabetes, and CKD.

Familial Hypercholesterolemia

  • FH is an inherited LDL-clearing disorder, commonly autosomal dominant, where LDL receptor function is reduced or absent.
  • High circulating LDL from early life increases arterial LDL retention, oxidation, foam-cell formation, plaque burden, and premature coronary disease.
  • Heterozygous FH is much more common than homozygous FH; homozygous disease can cause severe childhood cholesterol elevation and very early MI.
  • Clinical clues: tendon xanthomas, xanthelasma, corneal arcus at a young age, strong family history, and very high LDL.
  • Treatment logic: statins reduce hepatic cholesterol synthesis, ezetimibe reduces intestinal absorption, PCSK9 inhibitors increase LDL receptor recycling, and severe homozygous disease may require LDL apheresis.

Folic Acid and Congenital Heart Disease

  • The heart begins developing very early; primitive heart tube formation and beating occur around week 3, often before pregnancy is recognized.
  • Cardiac looping, septation, outflow tract division, valve formation, aortic arch development, and conduction-system development occur during the early embryonic period.
  • Folic acid supports one-carbon metabolism, DNA/RNA synthesis, methylation, amino acid metabolism, and rapid embryonic/placental cell division.
  • Deficiency risk matters most before and during early pregnancy because the critical cardiac-development window is early.
  • Clinical prevention focus: preconception counselling, supplement access, medication review, nutrition assessment, smoking cessation, and high-risk dosing when indicated by prescriber guidance.

Clinical Practice Quick Cards

Chest Pain / ACS

  • Assess onset, provocation, quality, radiation, severity, duration, associated dyspnea/diaphoresis/nausea/syncope.
  • ECG and serial troponins distinguish unstable angina/NSTEMI/STEMI with clinical context.
  • Do not rely on "classic" chest pain only; diabetes, older age, and women may present atypically.

Heart Failure

  • Ask dyspnea, orthopnea, PND, fatigue, edema, weight gain, nocturia, appetite, cognition.
  • Assess JVP, crackles, edema, hepatomegaly, oxygenation, BP/HR, urine output, weight trend.
  • Link symptoms to left congestion, right congestion, and low output.

PAD/DVT

  • PAD: pulses, cap refill, skin temp, wounds, claudication distance, rest pain.
  • DVT: unilateral swelling, pain, warmth, risk factors, immobility, cancer, surgery, estrogen, prior clot.
  • PE concern: new dyspnea, pleuritic pain, tachycardia, hypoxia, syncope, hemoptysis.

Practice and Active Recall

1. What starts atherosclerotic plaque formation?
Endothelial injury/dysfunction, which allows LDL entry, inflammation, monocyte adhesion, and oxidized LDL-driven foam-cell formation.
2. Why is plaque rupture dangerous?
It exposes thrombogenic material, causing platelet activation, coagulation, thrombus formation, and acute vessel occlusion.
3. Why does ischemia cause ATP depletion?
Reduced oxygen impairs mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation, so ATP production falls.
4. Stable angina vs unstable angina?
Stable angina is predictable demand ischemia from fixed narrowing; unstable angina is ACS from plaque disruption/partial thrombus with new/worsening/rest symptoms.
5. Why does troponin rise in MI?
Necrotic myocardial cells lose membrane integrity and release cardiac troponin into blood.
6. Primary vs secondary hypertension?
Primary is multifactorial without a single cause; secondary has an identifiable cause such as renal, endocrine, sleep apnea, vascular, or medication-related disease.
7. PAD vs DVT: what is the vessel problem?
PAD is arterial supply obstruction; DVT is venous clot formation with embolization risk.
8. HFrEF vs HFpEF?
HFrEF is weak contraction/low EF; HFpEF is stiff filling/relaxation problem with preserved EF.
9. Which congenital defects are cyanotic?
Tricuspid atresia and tetralogy of Fallot are classically cyanotic because systemic blood receives deoxygenated blood.
10. Why does PDA often cause bounding pulses?
Runoff from aorta into pulmonary artery lowers diastolic pressure and widens pulse pressure.
